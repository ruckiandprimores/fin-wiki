# Defensive Investor Portfolio Policy

> **TL;DR**: Split your portfolio 50/50 between bonds and stocks (adjustable between 25/75 and 75/25 based on conditions). Rebalance when the ratio drifts. Don't let the market's mood dictate your allocation — use mechanical rules to override your emotions.

## The Two Types of Investors

Graham divides investors by **effort**, not by risk tolerance:

| Type | Defining Trait | Goal | Time Required |
|------|----------------|------|---------------|
| **Defensive (Passive)** | Wants safety + freedom from bother | Don't lose; simplicity | Minimal |
| **Enterprising (Active)** | Willing to spend time on selection | Beat average return | Substantial |

> "The rate of return sought should be dependent, rather, on the amount of intelligent effort the investor is willing and able to bring to bear on his task."

**Key insight:** Most people should be defensive. The enterprising approach requires genuine skill and effort; Graham is skeptical most people have it.

## The 50/50 Baseline

Graham's core recommendation:

> "Maintain as nearly as practicable an equal division between bond and stock holdings."

**The rules:**
- Never less than 25% or more than 75% in stocks
- Same inverse range (75%–25%) for bonds
- 50/50 is the simplest "all-purpose" default

**When to adjust from 50/50:**
- **Increase stocks (toward 75%)** — if stocks are clearly cheap by value standards
- **Decrease stocks (toward 25%)** — if the market is "dangerously high"

But Graham immediately warns: these adjustments are "always difficult to follow — because they go against that very human nature which produces that excesses of bull and bear markets."

## Rebalancing Mechanics

> "When changes in the market level have raised the common-stock component to, say, 55%, the balance would be restored by a sale of one-eleventh of the stock portfolio and the transfer of the proceeds to bonds. Conversely, a fall in the common-stock proportion to 45% would call for the use of one-eleventh of the bond fund to buy additional equities."

**Practical implementation:**
1. Set your target (e.g., 50/50)
2. Check quarterly or semi-annually
3. If allocation drifts 5%+ from target, rebalance
4. Use fixed dates (New Year's, July 4th) to remove emotion

**Why this works:**
- Forces you to sell what's risen (stocks high = sell some)
- Forces you to buy what's fallen (stocks low = buy more)
- Removes prediction from the process

## Why Not 100% Stocks?

Graham addresses this directly in the commentary:

> "If you can act as a cold-blooded weigher of the odds, he would be likely to favor the low 25% stock component at this time, with the idea of waiting until conditions favor stocks."

**The 100% stocks test** — you qualify only if ALL these are true:
- Have 1+ year of cash reserves outside investments
- Will invest steadily for 20+ years
- Survived a bear market without selling
- Actually bought more during that bear market
- Have a formal plan to control your behavior

**Most people fail this test.** The bond cushion gives you courage to hold stocks when they crash.

## Bond Selection

Graham's bond principles (updated by Jason Zweig):

### Taxable vs. Tax-Free

Compare after-tax yields:
- If your marginal tax rate > 30%, municipal bonds often win
- Calculate: muni yield ÷ (1 - tax rate) = taxable equivalent

### Short vs. Long Term

| Type | Pro | Con |
|------|-----|-----|
| **Short-term** | Price stability | Lower yield |
| **Long-term** | Higher yield | Price swings with rates |

**Graham's preference:** Intermediate bonds (5-10 years) balance yield and stability.

### Bond Quality

- Stick to high-grade (AAA, AA, A)
- Lower-grade "junk" bonds require special expertise
- For individuals: bond funds beat individual bonds (diversification, convenience)

## U.S. Savings Bonds: A Special Case

Graham was a strong advocate of savings bonds for small investors:

> "There is no other investment that combines (1) absolute assurance of principal and interest payments, (2) the right to demand full 'money back' at any time, and (3) guarantee of at least a 5% interest rate for at least ten years."

Modern equivalent: **TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities)** offer similar guarantees with inflation protection.

## The Psychological Advantage

The deepest reason for the 50/50 rule isn't mathematical — it's behavioral:

> "The chief advantage, perhaps, is that such a formula will give him something to do. As the market advances he will from time to time make sales out of his stockholdings, putting the proceeds into bonds; as it declines he will reverse the procedure. These activities will provide some outlet for his otherwise too-pent-up energies."

**Translation:** Investors need to feel active. The rebalancing ritual satisfies this urge while keeping them from doing something stupid.

> "If he is the right kind of investor he will take added satisfaction from the thought that his operations are exactly opposite from those of the crowd."

## Why Age Isn't the Key Factor

Conventional wisdom: subtract age from 100 to get your stock %.

**Graham's view:** Wrong framework. What matters is:
- Your financial circumstances (income stability, obligations)
- Your emotional temperament (can you watch stocks drop 50%?)
- Your need for current income vs. growth
- Your time horizon for *specific goals*

An 89-year-old with $3M and grandchildren should own stocks (the grandchildren will inherit). A 25-year-old saving for a house should not (needs money soon).

## For Crypto (Adaptation)

The principles transfer, even if the specific allocations don't:

1. **Split exposure** — don't be 100% crypto or 0% crypto if you believe in the asset class
2. **Rebalance mechanically** — take profits when crypto moons, buy when it crashes
3. **Don't let euphoria/panic drive allocation** — set rules in advance
4. **Know your temperament** — if you can't handle 80% drawdowns, size accordingly

The biggest behavioral trap in crypto is going all-in at the top and panic-selling at the bottom — exactly what Graham's mechanical rebalancing prevents.

## Key Quotes

> "It is almost a contradiction in terms to suggest as a feasible policy for the average stockowner that he lighten his holdings when the market advances beyond a certain point and add to them after a corresponding decline. It is because the average man operates, and apparently must operate, in opposite fashion that we have had the great advances and collapses of the past."

> "A truly conservative investor will be satisfied with the gains shown on half his portfolio in a rising market, while in a severe decline he may derive much solace from reflecting how much better off he is than many of his more venturesome friends."

> "For most investors, intermediate bonds are the simplest choice, since they enable you to get out of the game of guessing what interest rates will do."

## Tension with Lynch (flagged 2026-04-29)

Lynch's "water the weeds" rule in [[investment/allocation/lynch-portfolio-design]] tells you to average up on winners and cut losers — the exact opposite of Graham's "trim what's risen, top up what's fallen" mechanical rebalancing.

**Resolution:** the rules operate at different levels.

- **Graham (here)**: rebalance the **bond/stock asset-class split** mechanically. The 50/50 rule is about *asset allocation*, not specific equity positions.
- **Lynch (there)**: *within the equity sleeve*, position-level decisions are thesis-driven, not mean-reversion-driven. Compounders are ridden; deteriorating positions are cut.

Both can coexist at different scales. The deeper question — whether *individual stocks* mean-revert or trend — is resolved in [[foundation/market-wisdom/lynch-six-categories|six-categories]]: stalwarts at the top of their normal P/E band do mean-revert (and Lynch's own [[investment/allocation/when-to-sell-by-category|sell rule]] for stalwarts agrees with Graham — trim them); fast growers in expansion phase do not (Lynch's "watering" applies). **Per-category, not blanket** for individual stocks; **mechanical** for asset allocation.
