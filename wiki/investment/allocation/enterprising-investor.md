# The Enterprising Investor

> **TL;DR**: The enterprising investor is willing to devote time and effort to security selection in hopes of beating average returns. This is harder than most think. The main paths: secondary companies at bargain prices, special situations, and—with extreme caution—growth stocks. Most people should be defensive investors instead.

## The Definition

Graham divides investors by effort, not by risk tolerance:

| Defensive | Enterprising |
|-----------|--------------|
| Safety + freedom from bother | Willing to spend time selecting |
| Goal: don't lose; simplicity | Goal: beat average return |
| Accepts market return | Seeks additional compensation for effort |

> "The rate of return sought should be dependent on the amount of intelligent effort the investor is willing and able to bring to bear on his task."

**Critical warning:**

> "We have some doubt whether a really substantial extra recompense is promised to the active investor under today's conditions."

Most people should be defensive. The enterprising approach requires genuine skill, effort, and temperament.

## The Negative Side: What to Avoid

Before discussing what to do, Graham lists what the enterprising investor should NOT do:

### 1. High-Grade Preferred Stocks (for individuals)

Better held by corporations (tax advantages). Individuals should stick to bonds.

### 2. Inferior Bonds and Preferreds

Higher yield comes with higher risk. The extra yield rarely compensates for defaults.

### 3. Foreign Government Bonds

Weak enforcement mechanisms. No recourse if a country defaults.

### 4. New Issues in General

IPOs are sold, not bought. The seller (investment bank) has superior information. On average, new issues underperform.

> "The intelligent investor should stay away from new issues if possible."

### 5. New Issues of Common Stock (especially hot markets)

The worst category. IPO pricing is set to benefit the seller. Initial pops are followed by long underperformance.

**The 1960-1962 and 1968-1970 patterns repeated:**
- Speculative IPOs soar on euphoria
- Crash when reality sets in
- Most never recover to their issue price

## The Positive Side: What to Do

Three strategies for the enterprising investor:

### 1. The "Relatively Unpopular Large Company"

Buy good companies when they're temporarily out of favor:

> "The market scolds big companies for short-term disappointments but lavishes small ones with unfounded adoration."

**Example (Graham's era):** DJIA "dogs" — the lowest-priced, highest-yield stocks in the index.

**Method:**
- Look for large, stable companies
- That have had disappointing recent results
- Whose stock prices reflect excessive pessimism
- Buy the 6-10 most unpopular

**Why it works:** Temporary problems at large companies are usually fixed. The market overreacts to bad news.

### 2. Bargain Issues

Buy stocks selling below their intrinsic value:

**Two types:**

#### a) Appraisal method
Estimate earnings power, multiply by appropriate factor, get intrinsic value. Buy at significant discount.

#### b) Net current asset value (NCAV)

> "The type of bargain issue that can be most readily identified is a common stock that sells for less than the company's net working capital alone, after deducting all prior obligations."

**Calculation:**
- Current assets minus ALL liabilities (including long-term)
- If stock price < this number, it's a statistical bargain

**Graham's experience:**
> "We followed this approach in the operations of Graham-Newman Corporation... and I can report that in 30 years the average annual return was about 20%."

**Difficulty:** These are rare. May not exist in bull markets.

### 3. Special Situations

Arbitrage and workout opportunities:

- Merger arbitrage (announced deals, stock prices not yet converged)
- Liquidations (company worth more dead than alive)
- Spin-offs and reorganizations
- Tender offers

**Requirements:**
- Specialized knowledge
- Ability to calculate risk/reward precisely
- Patience to wait for opportunities
- Willingness to accept many small gains

> "In each case the professional operator is risking a small loss if the outcome is unfavorable but is almost certain to make a sizable profit if the operation succeeds."

**Warning:** This was easier in Graham's era. Today, hedge funds dominate these situations with faster execution and more capital.

## Growth Stocks: A Cautionary Tale

Graham is deeply skeptical of growth stock investing:

> "Obviously prospects for physical growth in a business do not translate into obvious profits for investors."

**The problem:**
1. Growth stocks sell at high multiples
2. High multiples mean you're paying for future growth
3. If growth doesn't materialize (or is even slightly less than expected), the stock crashes
4. Even if growth happens, you may not profit — you already paid for it

**His airline example:**
- Everyone knew air travel would grow spectacularly in the 1950s-60s
- They were right — air traffic exploded
- Airline stocks lost money for investors — overcapacity, price wars, thin margins

> "The expert does not really select good companies for the layman... he picks the companies most likely to do well in stock price in a short future period."

### Formula Investing Failures

Zweig's update documents the failures:

**The Foolish Four:**
- Buy 4 DJIA stocks with highest dividend/price ratios
- Doubled up on second-highest
- Claimed 10% outperformance
- **Reality:** Based on random data mining. Failed immediately after publication.

**O'Shaughnessy's "What Works on Wall Street":**
- Claimed 18% annual returns from mechanical strategies
- Got a patent on his formula
- Launched mutual funds
- **Reality:** Funds massively underperformed. O'Shaughnessy left. "What Used to Work..."

> "All mechanical formulas for earning higher stock performance are 'a kind of self-destructive process — akin to the law of diminishing returns.'"

## The Core Problem

> "If the reason for selling stocks is that many of them are making losses and the outlook for business appears to be gloomy, the analyst will be inclined to question whether current prices are truly attractive. Conversely, if stocks have been sold because of current pessimism—which he may consider excessive—his very ability to recognize the excessiveness will constitute a favorable indication for investment."

**Translation:** The best bargains appear when things look worst. Most people can't pull the trigger then. The enterprising approach requires *acting* contrary to emotions, not just *thinking* contrarily.

## The Effort Question

Graham's honest assessment:

> "To obtain better than average investment results over a long pull requires a policy of selection or operation possessing a twofold merit: (1) It must meet objective or rational tests of underlying soundness; and (2) it must be different from the policy followed by most investors or speculators."

Both requirements are hard:
- Rational tests require real analysis (not pattern-matching)
- Being different requires psychological independence

Most people fail one or both. That's why most should be defensive.

## For Crypto (Adaptation)

**Strategies that might transfer:**

1. **Relatively unpopular large protocols** — Buy established L1s/L2s during temporary FUD
2. **Bargain issues** — Hard to define; maybe TVL/market cap ratios below historical norms?
3. **Special situations** — Airdrops, token migrations, exchange listings, protocol mergers

**What definitely applies:**

> "Speculating when you think you are investing" — most "enterprising" crypto activity is speculation dressed up as research.

## Key Quotes

> "Investment is most intelligent when it is most businesslike."

> "To achieve satisfactory investment results is easier than most people realize; to achieve superior results is harder than it looks."

> "The investor's chief problem — and even his worst enemy — is likely to be himself."

> "Even the intelligent investor is likely to need considerable willpower to keep from following the crowd."

## Tension with Lynch (flagged 2026-04-29)

Lynch's framework celebrates "fast growers" as his favorite category — 10- to 40-baggers, sometimes 200-baggers. Graham's stance here ("obvious prospects for physical growth do not translate into obvious profits") looks contradictory.

**Resolution:** Graham's warning is specifically about **paying for growth that's already priced in.** The airline example: everyone knew air travel would grow → prices priced it in → investors lost despite the growth materializing. Lynch's discipline (PEG-style: never pay materially more for growth than the growth rate itself justifies) actually obeys Graham's rule — Lynch is buying growth at a *discount* to its compound value, not at the consensus premium.

**Deeper resolution (added 2026-04-29 after Buffett ingest):** [[foundation/market-wisdom/value-vs-growth-false-dichotomy|Buffett's framing]] dissolves the dichotomy entirely. Growth and value are not separate strategies — growth is a *variable in the value calculation*. The airline case (Graham's warning) is value-destructive growth: airline industry growth required capex earning below cost of capital, so growth subtracted value even as passenger miles increased. The Wal-Mart case (Lynch's enthusiasm) is value-creating growth: each new store earned returns far above cost of capital, so growth multiplied value. Both authors are saying the same thing in different cases: **the question is not whether growth is happening, but whether growth occurs at returns above cost of capital.**
