# Defensive Investor Stock Selection Criteria

> **TL;DR**: Graham's 7 quantitative criteria for selecting stocks: adequate size, strong finances, earnings stability, dividend track record, earnings growth, moderate P/E, and moderate price-to-book. Most stocks fail; that's the point.

## The Philosophy

Graham offers two approaches for the defensive investor:

1. **DJIA-type portfolio** — buy a cross-section of leading stocks (today: index fund)
2. **Quantitatively-tested portfolio** — apply strict criteria to filter quality at reasonable prices

The second approach requires more effort but allows some personal selection within bounds.

## The Seven Criteria

### 1. Adequate Size of Enterprise

> "Not less than $100 million of annual sales for an industrial company."

**Why:** Small companies face more volatility and business risk. The defensive investor wants boring stability.

**Modern equivalent:** ~$2B+ market cap, or membership in S&P 500.

### 2. Sufficiently Strong Financial Condition

**For industrials:**
- Current ratio ≥ 2.0 (current assets / current liabilities)
- Long-term debt ≤ net current assets (working capital)

**For utilities:**
- Debt ≤ 2× stock equity (book value)

**Why:** Companies that can't pay their bills eventually go bankrupt. Financial strength provides margin of safety.

### 3. Earnings Stability

> "Some earnings for the common stock in each of the past ten years."

No losses allowed in any of the past 10 years.

**Why:** Companies that can maintain profitability through multiple economic cycles are more predictable.

### 4. Dividend Record

> "Uninterrupted payments for at least the past 20 years."

**Why:** Long dividend history demonstrates:
- Real cash generation (dividends can't be faked like earnings)
- Management commitment to shareholders
- Business stability across cycles

### 5. Earnings Growth

> "A minimum increase of at least one-third in per-share earnings in the past ten years using three-year averages at the beginning and end."

Calculation:
- Average EPS for most recent 3 years vs. average EPS for years 8-10 ago
- Growth must be ≥ 33%

**Why:** Without growth, you're buying a business that's treading water (or worse, declining when adjusted for inflation).

### 6. Moderate Price/Earnings Ratio

> "Current price should not be more than 15 times average earnings of the past three years."

The inverse (earnings/price ratio) should at least equal the high-grade bond rate.

**Example (2003):**
- AA bond yield: 4.6%
- Required E/P: 4.6%
- Max P/E: 100 ÷ 4.6 = 21.7

**Why:** High P/E means you're paying for future growth that may not materialize. Lower P/E provides [[foundation/market-wisdom/margin-of-safety|margin of safety]].

### 7. Moderate Ratio of Price to Assets

> "Current price should not be more than 1½ times the book value last reported."

**Graham's combined rule:**
- P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5

This allows tradeoffs:
- P/E of 15 × P/B of 1.5 = 22.5 ✓
- P/E of 9 × P/B of 2.5 = 22.5 ✓
- P/E of 6 × P/B of 3.0 = 18.0 ✓

**Why:** Book value provides a floor. Paying too far above it leaves no margin if the business deteriorates.

## Summary Table

| Criterion | Rule | Purpose |
|-----------|------|---------|
| 1. Size | $100M+ sales | Avoid small-cap volatility |
| 2. Financials | CR ≥ 2.0, debt < working capital | Avoid bankruptcy risk |
| 3. Stability | Earnings every year for 10 years | Avoid cyclical disasters |
| 4. Dividends | 20+ years uninterrupted | Prove cash generation |
| 5. Growth | EPS +33% over 10 years (3-yr avg) | Avoid stagnation |
| 6. P/E | ≤ 15 (or adjusted for bond yields) | Avoid overpaying |
| 7. P/B | ≤ 1.5 (or P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5) | Asset-backed value |

## How Many Stocks Pass?

When Graham applied all seven criteria to the DJIA 30 in 1970:

> "Only five of them would meet all our requirements: American Can, American Tel. & Tel., Anaconda, Swift, and Woolworth."

And for the broader market:

> "About 100 issues of this sort could have been found in the Standard & Poor's Stock Guide at the end of 1970."

**This is by design.** The criteria are meant to be strict. Graham wants you to fish in a small pond of quality, not the ocean of mediocrity.

## The Two Camps: Protection vs. Prediction

Graham identifies two approaches to stock analysis:

| Prediction (Qualitative) | Protection (Quantitative) |
|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| Emphasize future prospects | Emphasize current value |
| Focus on growth projections | Focus on measurable margins |
| "This company will dominate" | "At this price, I'm protected" |
| Requires being right about future | Can afford to be partially wrong |

Graham clearly favors protection:

> "We were not willing to accept the prospects and promises of the future as compensation for a lack of sufficient value in hand."

## Public Utilities: The "Solution"

Graham noted that utilities in his era satisfied defensive criteria more easily:
- Stable earnings (regulated monopolies)
- Consistent dividends
- Reasonable P/E and P/B ratios
- Lower volatility than industrials

**Modern caution:** Utility deregulation and nuclear plant debacles have made this sector less safe than in Graham's day. Own via diversified funds if at all.

## The Selectivity Problem

Can you beat a simple index by selecting the "best" stocks?

Graham is skeptical:

> "If one could select the best stocks unerringly, one would only lose by diversifying."

And on analyst predictions:

> "Suppose we had asked a hundred security analysts to choose the 'best' five stocks in the Dow. Few would have come up with identical choices and many of the lists would have differed completely."

**His conclusion:** The current price already reflects known facts and average expectations. To beat the market, you need to be:
- Consistently right when consensus is wrong
- Disciplined enough to act on your analysis

Most defensive investors should just diversify and stop trying.

## For Crypto (Adaptation Problem)

Graham's criteria are fundamentally **inapplicable** to most crypto:

| Criterion | Problem in Crypto |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Size | Market cap is manipulable; no "sales" |
| Financial strength | Protocols don't have balance sheets |
| Earnings stability | Most tokens have no earnings |
| Dividend record | Staking yields ≠ dividends |
| Earnings growth | No earnings to grow |
| P/E | No earnings for denominator |
| P/B | No book value |

**What might transfer:**
- The *spirit* of quantitative screening over narrative-driven selection
- Network metrics (TVL, fees, users) as rough analogs to fundamentals
- Skepticism of "growth story" valuations without current value

**Open question:** Can we construct Graham-style criteria using on-chain metrics? (e.g., fees/market cap as "P/E", TVL/market cap as "P/B")

## Key Quotes

> "Every competent analyst looks forward to the future rather than backward to the past... Nevertheless, the future itself can be approached in two different ways: the way of prediction and the way of protection."

> "Those who emphasize prediction will endeavor to anticipate fairly accurately just what the company will accomplish... Those who emphasize protection are always especially concerned with the price of the issue at the time of study."

> "In our own attitude and professional work we were always committed to the quantitative approach. From the first we wanted to make sure that we were getting ample value for our money in concrete, demonstrable terms."

> "Our advice to the defensive investor is that he let it [selectivity] alone. Let him emphasize diversification more than individual selection."
