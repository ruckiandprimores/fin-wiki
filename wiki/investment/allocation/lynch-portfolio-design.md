# Lynch's Portfolio Design — Categories, Concentration, and Watering

> **TL;DR**: Lynch deliberately rejects standard diversification logic. His operational rules: own as many stocks as you can find at attractive prices in different categories; don't dilute fast growers with slow growers; "water the weeds" (his term for averaging up on winners and cutting losers) — the opposite of mean-reversion instinct. The framework is empirical not theoretical and works only if combined with category-specific buy/sell rules.

## How Many Stocks?

Lynch's answer is contrarian. Most equity-investing literature argues for concentration ("diworsification by overdiversification"). Lynch ran Magellan with 1,400+ stocks at peak.

> "When I saw a bargain I couldn't resist buying it, and in those days there were bargains everywhere... Buying hundreds of stocks certainly wasn't Ned Johnson's idea of how to run an equity fund, but I'm still here."

His operational rationale:
- Number of holdings = number of *attractive opportunities you've found at the moment*, not a theoretical optimum
- Each stock is *its own thesis*; the portfolio doesn't need a sector mix because each thesis stands alone
- Owning 150 S&Ls (his peak in that category) was fine because each had passed individual screening

**For amateurs**, Lynch suggests a more practical range:

> "In small portfolios I'd be comfortable owning between three and ten stocks."

The asymmetry: an amateur with 3-10 well-researched names is operating in their information edge; an amateur trying to track 100 names without dedicated time is just running a stale fund.

## Category Mix Rules

Lynch implicitly argues for a category mix to avoid concentration risk:

| Category | Role in portfolio | Suggested weighting |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Stalwarts | Recession protection, ballast | "Always keep some" |
| Fast growers | Return engine — multibaggers | Largest allocation if you can find them |
| Slow growers | Yield/dividend; rare in his portfolio | Minimal |
| Cyclicals | Cycle-timed; sized to your cycle conviction | Variable, often zero outside opportunity windows |
| Turnarounds | Asymmetric upside; "least related to market" | Always hold a few when available |
| Asset plays | Patient value capture | When the gap is found |

His personal preference: heavy on **fast growers and turnarounds** (the highest-multibagger categories), with stalwarts as ballast.

## Watering the Weeds — The Anti-Mean-Reversion Rule

> "Some people automatically sell the 'winners' — stocks that go up — and hold on to their 'losers' — stocks that go down — which is about as sensible as pulling out the flowers and watering the weeds."

Lynch's discipline:
- **Average up on winners** when the underlying business is still compounding
- **Cut losers** when the thesis has deteriorated
- The opposite of the natural human instinct (which is loss aversion: hold the loser hoping it recovers, sell the winner to "lock in profits")

The principle is *thesis-driven*, not price-driven:
- A winner up 200% with a still-intact thesis is a buy, not a sell
- A loser down 30% with deteriorated fundamentals is a sell, not a buy
- Mean-reversion ("it must come back") is the trap — see his Twelve Silliest Things list

**Caveat:** "watering the weeds" is correct *only* when combined with mechanism-grounded thesis tracking. Without that, it becomes momentum chasing — and momentum strategies are well-documented to mean-revert at the time horizons they were originally optimized for.

## When to Buy

Lynch identifies two especially good buying windows:

### 1. End-of-Year Tax-Loss Selling (October–December in U.S.)

> "Tax-driven selling pressure that hits stocks late in the year creates ideal buying opportunities."

Mechanism: investors with paper losses crystallize them for tax purposes by year-end. The selling pressure is *not* fundamentals-driven, so prices fall below thesis value temporarily. Recovery in January–February (the "January effect") is well-documented.

### 2. Market Crashes / Corrections / Scares

> "If you can summon the courage and presence of mind to buy during these scary episodes when your stomach says 'sell,' you'll find opportunities that you wouldn't have thought you'd ever see again."

The 1987 crash (October 19) — Lynch returned from Ireland to a 22% one-day market drop. His response: buy. Companies' fundamentals didn't change in one day; their stock prices did.

> "Professionals are often too busy or too constrained to act quickly in market breaks, but look at the solid companies with excellent earnings growth that you could have picked up in the latest ones."

**The amateur's structural advantage during crashes:** no investment committee, no quarterly review, no client redemptions to manage. The amateur can buy when the professional must sell.

## Honest Caveats and Limits

- **Lynch ran Magellan; you don't.** Some of his rules (1,400 holdings) are mutual-fund-scale rules. The amateur shouldn't try to mirror Magellan.
- **The "watering the weeds" rule has tail risk.** Averaging up on winners with momentum *until* mechanism breaks down is procyclical — it makes max-drawdowns deeper than equal-weighting. Position limits matter.
- **Buy-the-crash is correct on average and disastrous on tails.** 1987 was a great buying opportunity; 1929–1932 was not (you'd have caught a falling knife for 89% of the way down). Lynch acknowledges this implicitly via his "fundamentals matter, not the calendar" framing.
- **The Magellan record was achieved before crypto, before passive flow dominance, and before retail option trading.** The institutional lag he exploited is partially closed. The amateur edge today is most likely in markets institutional capital has not yet fully indexed (small-cap, OTC, frontier markets, crypto).

## Crypto Adaptation

### Number of holdings

For crypto, Lynch's "3 to 10 names for amateurs" suggestion translates with adjustment:
- **High idiosyncratic risk** in crypto means more diversification is appropriate at the small-position level
- 5–15 names with conviction-weighted sizing is a defensible range
- Treasury / cold-storage allocation (BTC + ETH) operates at a separate layer

### Category mix in crypto

Hypothesized mix (needs validation):
- **BTC/ETH (stalwart-equivalents)** — recession protection role; ballast
- **Top revenue-generating protocols (stalwart-equivalents)** — cash-flow proxies
- **New L1/L2/protocol fast growers** — multibagger upside; size small per name due to mortality risk
- **Cyclical positioning** — long-only or rotational depending on cycle phase
- **Turnaround opportunities** — post-exploit, post-bear-market revivals
- **Asset plays** — DAOs trading below treasury

### Watering the weeds in crypto

Stronger application than in equities, because:
- Network effects are stronger (winner-take-most dynamics in many crypto verticals)
- Mortality of weak protocols is higher (cutting losers earlier saves more)

But also stronger trap:
- Memecoin runs that look like fast-grower compounders are pure narrative
- "Winning" tokens can dump on unlocks even with strong narrative
- Higher need for thesis-discipline before averaging up

## Key Takeaways

- Number of holdings = number of attractive theses you've found, not a fixed N
- For amateurs: 3–10 (equities) or 5–15 (crypto) is reasonable
- Category mix matters: stalwarts as ballast, fast growers as engine, turnarounds for asymmetric upside
- "Water the weeds" — average up on winners with intact thesis, cut losers with broken thesis
- Tax-loss season and market crashes are the amateur's best entry windows
- The amateur's structural advantage is most visible during crises — no committee, no redemptions, no benchmark

## Tension with Graham (flagged 2026-04-29)

Lynch's "water the weeds" rule (average up on winners, cut losers) directly contradicts Graham's mechanical rebalancing rule in [[investment/allocation/defensive-investor-portfolio]] (sell what's risen, buy what's fallen). Surface reading: pure contradiction.

**Resolution:** the rules apply at **different abstraction levels and to different things.**

- Graham's mechanical rebalancing operates at the **asset-class level** (bonds ↔ stocks). Trim the over-allocated asset class, not specific holdings within it.
- Lynch's "water the weeds" operates at the **individual-position level within the equity sleeve.** Within "I should hold 60% equities," the question of *which equities* is governed by thesis-tracking, not mean-reversion.

You can do both: mechanically rebalance the bond/stock split (Graham), while within the stock sleeve, ride compounders and cut deteriorating positions (Lynch). The deeper philosophical disagreement — *do equity prices mean-revert or trend?* — is partially resolved by [[foundation/market-wisdom/lynch-six-categories|category]]: stalwarts at the top of their P/E band do mean-revert (Lynch's own [[investment/allocation/when-to-sell-by-category|sell rule]] for stalwarts is a form of trim-the-winner that Graham would endorse); fast growers in mid-expansion do not (Lynch's "watering" applies). **Per-category, not blanket.**

## Open Questions

- **What is the optimal number of crypto positions for an amateur with day-job constraints?** Lynch's principle is observation-driven; how many tokens can a single observer monitor in real-time?
- **Does the "buy the crash" rule survive the leverage characteristics of crypto?** Crashes in crypto are often correlated with cascading liquidations; "fundamentals haven't changed" may not hold.
