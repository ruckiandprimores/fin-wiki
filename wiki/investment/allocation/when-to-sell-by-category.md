# When to Sell, By Category — Lynch's Exit Rules

> **TL;DR**: Lynch refuses to give a single "sell rule" because each of his [[foundation/market-wisdom/lynch-six-categories|six stock categories]] requires different exit logic. Selling a fast grower at +50% costs you the next 5×; selling a stalwart at +50% is correct. Selling a cyclical at high earnings is correct; selling it at low earnings is the trap. The category determines the rule.

## The Meta-Rule

> "If you know why you bought a stock in the first place, you'll automatically have a better idea of when to say good-bye to it."

Lynch's framework forces you to write down, *at purchase*, why you bought:
- Slow grower → for the dividend and modest appreciation
- Stalwart → for recession protection and 30–50% appreciation
- Fast grower → for the multibagger as the formula expands
- Cyclical → for the cycle recovery
- Turnaround → for the post-distress re-rating
- Asset play → for the value gap to close (often via raider)

When the *reason* you bought is no longer present, sell. When the reason is still present, hold even through 30%+ drawdowns.

## Per-Category Sell Rules

### Slow Growers — Sell on +30–50% or Fundamental Deterioration

Lynch barely owns slow growers. When he does:
- Sell at 30–50% appreciation
- Sell on fundamental deterioration *even if the stock has fallen* (don't anchor to your cost)

**Specific sell signals:**
- Lost market share for two consecutive years and is hiring another advertising agency
- No new products being developed; R&D spending curtailed
- Two recent acquisitions of unrelated businesses (= [[glossary/diworseification]]) and announces it's looking for further acquisitions "at the leading edge of technology"
- Balance sheet has deteriorated from no debt + millions in cash → no cash + millions in debt
- At a lower stock price, the dividend yield still wouldn't attract investors — meaning the dividend itself isn't safe

### Stalwarts — Sell When P/E Strays Above the Historical Band

Stalwarts are *traded*, not *held* in Lynch's framework: "Stocks that I generally buy for a 30 to 50 percent gain, then sell and repeat the process with similar issues that haven't yet appreciated."

**Specific sell signals:**
- Stock price gets above the earnings line (visual P/E expansion)
- P/E strays beyond normal range — "P/E of 15, while similar-quality companies in the industry have P/E's of 11–12"
- New products in last two years had mixed results; future products a year+ away
- No officers/directors have bought shares in the last year
- Major division (25%+ of earnings) vulnerable to slump (housing, oil, etc.)
- Growth slowing; profits maintained only by cost-cutting; cost-cutting opportunities limited

**The Avon trap:** at 50× earnings, sell, even if the company is great. *(See chart, p. 175–176.)*

### Fast Growers — The Trick Is Not to Lose the Tenbagger

Lynch's most valuable category, where mistiming is most expensive. The principle: **sell when the second phase of growth is ending, not when the price has gone up a lot.**

> "If forty Wall Street analysts are giving the stock their highest recommendation, 60 percent of the shares are held by institutions, and three national magazines have fawned over the CEO, then it's definitely time to think about selling."

**Specific sell signals:**
- Same-store sales down 3% in the last quarter
- New store results disappointing (geographic expansion failing)
- Two top executives + several key employees leave for a rival firm
- Company recently returned from "dog and pony show" — telling its story to institutional investors in 12 cities in 2 weeks (= institutional saturation)
- All the characteristics of a Stock-You'd-Avoid (see [[foundation/market-wisdom/stocks-to-avoid-lynch]])
- P/E ≥ 30 with most-optimistic earnings projection of 15–20% (PEG > 1.5 implicit)

**The double-whammy warning:** when a fast grower's growth slows, both the P/E *and* the E compress. A 25% growth → 10% growth transition can drop a stock 70% even with rising earnings.

**The Holiday Inn check:** "When you saw a Holiday Inn franchise every twenty miles along every major U.S. highway, and then you traveled to Gibraltar and saw a Holiday Inn at the base of the rock, it had to be time to worry. Where else could they expand? Mars?"

### Cyclicals — Sell at Cycle Peaks, Not at Price Peaks

This is the most counterintuitive category. *(Full mechanism in [[trading/mechanisms/cyclical-timing]].)* Sell signals:

- **Inventories building up** that can't be sold ("when the parking lot is full of ingots")
- **Falling commodity prices** — oil, steel, etc. roll over months before earnings
- **Future commodity prices < spot** — futures market predicting weakness
- **Capacity additions** — capital spending budget doubled to build new plants
- **Two key union contracts expiring in next 12 months, labor demanding wage restoration**
- **Final demand for the product slowing**
- **New foreign producers entering** — competitive entry compressing margins

**The defense-cyclical example:** General Dynamics fell 50% on *higher* earnings — the knowledgeable vanguard sold a year ahead of the news.

### Turnarounds — Sell When It Has Turned Around (and Reclassify)

> "The best time to sell a turnaround is after it's turned around. All the troubles are over and everybody knows it."

When the company is no longer in distress, it's no longer a turnaround — it's the new category (cyclical / stalwart / fast grower / etc.). The category-specific rules then apply to the *new* category.

**Chrysler example:** at $2 / $5 / $10 (post-bankruptcy distress), it was a turnaround. At $48 in 1987, it was a recovered cyclical auto company — and should be evaluated/exited as a cyclical, not held as a turnaround.

**Specific sell signals (still in turnaround mode):**
- Debt that had declined for 5 straight quarters just rose by $25M (deterioration)
- Inventories rising at twice the rate of sales growth
- P/E inflated relative to earnings prospects
- Strongest division (50% of output) sells to one customer who's slowing — middleman risk

### Asset Plays — Wait for the Raider

Modern (post-1980s) asset plays usually realize value through a takeover or shareholder activist. Lynch:

> "You don't sell until the Bass brothers show up, and if it's not the Bass brothers, then it's certain to be Steinberg, Icahn, the Belzbergs, the Pritzkers, Irwin Jacobs, Sir James Goldsmith, Donald Trump, Boone Pickens, or maybe even Merv Griffin."

Once a raider arrives, expect bidding war / takeover / LBO → 2–4× pop.

**Specific sell signals:**
- Shares sell at a discount to real market value, but management announces a 10% share issuance to fund a diversification — value is being destroyed
- A division expected to sell for $20M only fetches $12M in actual sale (asset value below estimates)
- Reduction in corporate tax rate considerably reduces value of tax-loss carryforward
- Institutional ownership has risen from 25% → 60% in 5 years — the asset story is now consensus and the gap closes from buying, not from value realization

## What NOT to Sell On

Lynch's "Twelve Silliest Things People Say About Stock Prices" (Ch 18) catalogs the bad reasons:

| Bad reason | Why it's wrong |
|------------|----------------|
| "It's gone down this much, can't go lower" | Polaroid: $143 → $14, *every step* triggered this rationalization |
| "If it's gone this high, can't go higher" | Wal-Mart at $4 went to $40, then to $400 |
| "Eventually they always come back" | RCA: 65 years and counting |
| "It's only $3 a share, what can I lose?" | All of it, exactly the same as a $300 stock if it goes to zero |
| "It's always darkest before dawn" | Sometimes it's darkest before pitch black |
| "When it rebounds to $10, I'll sell" | Doesn't account for time value, opportunity cost, or whether $10 is rational |
| "Conservative stocks don't fluctuate much" | Bristol-Myers at recession bottoms, Avon -80% |
| "It's taking too long" | The Limited took 7 years from IPO to first analyst — patience is the moat |
| "Look at all the money I've lost — I didn't buy it!" | Pure regret bias; invest looking forward, not backward |
| "I missed that one, I'll catch the next one" | The "next IBM" is the curse — see [[foundation/market-wisdom/stocks-to-avoid-lynch]] |
| "Stock's gone up, I must be right" | The stock going up is not evidence the thesis is right (= momentum trap) |

The unifying theme: **never use price action alone as the sell signal.** Sell on *thesis change*, not on *price change*.

## Crypto Adaptation

The category-specific rule structure transfers cleanly. The signals require crypto-specific instrumentation.

### Crypto Sell Signals by Category

**Crypto stalwart sell** (BTC/ETH-like):
- Fee revenue declining quarter-over-quarter while price rising
- Realized cap declining (long-term holders distributing)
- Major upgrades repeatedly delayed (execution risk emerging)

**Crypto fast grower sell** (new L1/L2/dapp post-launch):
- Active address growth decelerating below 5% MoM
- TVL growth outpaced by token unlocks (cosmetic growth)
- Major team members leaving for competitors
- Magazine/podcast coverage saturated; CT consensus = bullish
- FDV / sales > 100 (PEG analog)

**Crypto cyclical sell** (almost everything):
- Funding strongly positive; basis premium >5% annualized for weeks
- Stablecoin supply growth flat-to-declining
- Capex equivalent: validators/miners adding capacity at peak prices
- Mainstream press / non-crypto Twitter discovering crypto

**Crypto turnaround sell** (post-exploit recovery):
- Pre-incident usage levels reattained → reclassify as the original category
- New issuance to fund recovery (the Navistar warning) → exit
- Insurance/bug-bounty payments depleted; balance sheet deteriorated

**Crypto asset play sell** (treasury < market cap DAOs):
- Activist buys reaching 5%+ stake
- Treasury redistribution proposal passes
- *Don't* sell on the value gap closing without a catalyst — that's just being paid to wait

## Key Takeaways

- Single sell rule = wrong; category-specific rule = right
- Slow grower: +30-50% or fundamental deterioration
- Stalwart: P/E above historical band
- Fast grower: same-store/usage decline OR institutional saturation OR PEG > 1.5
- Cyclical: late-cycle signals (capacity, inventory, commodity rollover) — sell on cycle peak, not price peak
- Turnaround: when the turnaround completes — and reclassify
- Asset play: when the raider/activist arrives
- The meta-rule: sell on **thesis change**, not on **price change**
