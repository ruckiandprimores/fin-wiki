# Pharma Economics Primer — Patents, Cliffs, Biosimilars, PBMs

> **TL;DR**: Pharma is **the textbook moat industry** — patents grant temporary monopoly + regulatory barriers + scale + brand persistence. The unique structural feature: **patent cliff** (small molecules lose 70-80% of revenue 12-18 months after generic entry; biologics decline slower under biosimilar competition). Industry economics dominated by R&D risk-spread, lifecycle management, and the **PBM/formulary intermediation layer** that breaks the simple "lower price = more sales" assumption. **2025-2030 LOE wave** puts $200-400B in branded revenue at risk. Substrate page for any pharma investment thesis; user-CoC currently weak (per this research program value-investing entry plan) — this page builds CoC, doesn't validate a thesis.

## Why Pharma Has Wide Moats

Per [[foundation/market-wisdom/circle-of-competence|Buffett's framework]] applied to pharma, four moat layers compound:

### 1. Patents (the load-bearing moat)

- **Composition-of-matter patent**: 20 years from filing date; covers the molecule itself
- **Method-of-use patent**: covers specific therapeutic uses; can extend exclusivity
- **Formulation patent**: covers specific delivery mechanism (extended-release, transdermal, etc.)
- **Effective market exclusivity**: typically 8-14 years from launch (patents filed during R&D, lots of clock burns before approval)
- **Patent stack ("evergreening")**: layering composition + method + formulation patents extends de facto exclusivity beyond composition expiry

### 2. Regulatory Approval Barriers

- **NDA / BLA process**: 7-10 years + $1-3B average development cost
- **Generic ANDA**: $1-4M, 2-4 years (much lower bar)
- **Biosimilar approval**: $100-250M, 7-8 years (closer to original biologic in cost than to small-molecule generic)
- **FDA exclusivity period**: 5 years for new chemical entity (NCE), 7 years for orphan drug, 12 years for biologic — **independent of patents**

The regulatory layer means even if patent expires, no competitor enters the market until they navigate their own regulatory pathway. This creates an **8-14 year window of mostly-uncontested commercial life** post-launch.

### 3. Scale & Distribution

- Global sales force, regulatory affairs, manufacturing — high fixed cost; massive operating leverage on a successful drug
- Big pharma can fund 10-20 simultaneous trials; biotech can run 1-3
- **Implication**: even when patent expires, big pharma can **license-in next-generation drugs** from biotech, perpetuating revenue

### 4. Brand & Physician Relationships

- Once a drug becomes standard-of-care, prescriber switching costs are real (clinical experience, formulary inertia, patient-prescriber relationship)
- Branded retention persists post-LOE for some drugs (Lipitor: 50%+ revenue retained 24 months post-LOE due to brand + formulary placement)

### Buffett-Class Indicator Check

Per the search-derived Buffett framework: **ROE >15% sustained over 5+ years**, stable margins, high free cash flow generation. Big pharma typically:
- ROE 25-40% sustained
- Operating margins 25-35%
- Free cash flow / Revenue 25-30%
- Returns capital via dividend + buyback (similar to consumer-staples profile)

Pharma is on Buffett's "wide-moat-good-business" list when patent cliff is well-managed. **The risk is concentration of revenue in patent-cliffing assets**.

## The Patent Cliff (the structural risk)

### The cliff is the contract, not the bug (Kolchinsky framing)

Per [[foundation/sources/kolchinsky-great-american-drug-deal|Kolchinsky's "Biotech Social Contract"]]: branded prices are **mortgage payments society makes on a permanent asset** — the drug's eventual generic form. Genericization is the system's *designed payoff*: 89% of US prescriptions are generics costing ~$88B vs ~$237B for the branded 11% (2015); Lipitor fell ~95% at LOE and stayed there ("the mountain of generics").

**Valuation discipline**: model near-zero branded terminal value per asset at LOE — durable terminal value lives in the platform (pipeline replacement, M&A capability), not the molecule. A valuation implying branded pricing in perpetuity is priced against the contract, which has political enforcement teeth. Corollary red-flag screen (Kolchinsky's contract-breaker taxonomy): price-jacking off-patent sole-source drugs (Daraprim 55×, REMS-blocked bioequivalence), biosimilar-stalling (~$10B/yr), non-innovative rebranding — revenue built this way is regulatory-arbitrage rent harvesting political risk, not innovation.

### Small-Molecule Cliff Mechanics

**Standard pattern**: 70-80% of branded revenue lost within 12-18 months of first generic entry.

**Why so fast**:
- **Substitution at the pharmacy**: generics are required to be bioequivalent (FDA-defined ±20% pharmacokinetic equivalence); pharmacist substitutes generic when prescribed branded (in most US states, default is substitute)
- **PBM tier moves**: post-generic, PBMs move generic to preferred tier and demote branded to non-preferred (higher patient co-pay) — patient economics drive switching
- **Formulary exclusion**: some PBMs entirely exclude post-LOE branded drugs

**Magnitudes**:
- **Year 1 post-LOE**: 70-80% revenue lost (small molecule)
- **Year 2**: an additional 5-10%
- **Year 3+**: long-tail residual (some branded drugs retain a "premium prescriber" cohort)

### Biologic / Biosimilar Cliff Mechanics

**Slower and less complete** than small-molecule cliff because:
- **Biosimilar interchangeability** is harder — must demonstrate clinical equivalence, not just bioequivalence
- **Prescriber comfort with substitution** is lower (biologics are complex protein structures; clinicians prefer the originator)
- **PBM formulary dynamics** include biologic-specific rebate structures that can favor originator
- **Storage / handling differences** between originator and biosimilar require re-certification at clinic level
- **Manufacturing complexity** means fewer biosimilar manufacturers enter (vs hundreds of generic ANDA holders)

**Magnitudes** (Humira, Remicade, Avastin worked examples):
- **Year 1 post-LOE**: 20-40% revenue lost (biologic)
- **Year 2-3**: cumulative 50-70% lost
- **Long tail**: biologics often retain 30-50% market share 5+ years post-LOE due to substitution friction

**Investment implication**: a pharma company with patent cliffs in biologics has a **slower, more predictable revenue ramp-down** than one with small-molecule cliffs — easier to navigate via lifecycle management.

### The 2025-2030 LOE Wave

**$200-400B in branded drug revenue at LOE risk** per industry analyses. Major events:
- Keytruda (Merck) — biologic; LOE 2028
- Eliquis (BMS / Pfizer) — small molecule; LOE 2027-2028
- Stelara (J&J) — biologic; LOE 2025
- Opdivo (BMS) — biologic; LOE 2028
- Various Pfizer COVID-era products
- AbbVie post-Humira franchise (Humira itself LOE 2023, but lifecycle managed via Skyrizi/Rinvoq)

**Implication for pharma sector investing 2026-2030**: the LOE wave creates **dispersion** — companies that have lifecycle-managed (replaced LOE-cliffing assets with newer franchises) outperform companies that haven't. Stock-picking, not sector ETF, is the operative discipline.

## Lifecycle Management (extending exclusivity)

Pharma firms use multiple tactics to extend revenue beyond raw patent expiry:

### Patent Stack Extension

- File method-of-use + formulation patents during exclusivity period; covers next-gen dosing forms
- Create extended-release, combination products, pediatric formulations — each can carry its own patent + regulatory exclusivity

### Authorized Generics

- Originator licenses to a generic manufacturer pre-LOE; captures partial generic market share post-LOE while retaining premium-branded share
- Reduces the cliff from 70-80% loss to ~50-60% loss

### Indication Expansion

- Get approved for additional disease indications during exclusivity (cancer drug → autoimmune; antidepressant → chronic pain)
- Extends effective patent life via methods-of-use claims tied to new indication
- Often reset clock by 3-5 years on incremental revenue

### Next-Generation Successor

- Develop and launch successor drug 4-6 years before LOE; transition prescribers gradually
- AbbVie: Humira → Skyrizi/Rinvoq (the canonical successful transition)
- Counterexample: Pfizer Viagra → no clean successor; lost most franchise post-LOE

### M&A as Pipeline Replenishment

- Big pharma acquires biotech with late-stage assets to refresh pipeline
- Typical M&A multiple: 5-10× peak-sales for de-risked Phase III asset
- This is **the most reliable pharma capital-allocation pattern** — and a source of recurring returns to specialty biotech

## R&D Economics

### The Unit Economics

- **Average cost per approved drug (industry-wide)**: $1.5-2.5B (DiMasi 2016 + updates) including failed candidates
- **Phase I → Approval probability**: ~10-15% (varies by therapeutic area)
- **Phase III → Approval probability**: ~50-60%
- **Average development time**: 10-12 years from discovery to launch

### Implication: Pipeline Diversification

A pharma firm needs **5-10 simultaneous late-stage candidates** to expect 1-2 launches per year. Concentrated pipelines (1-2 assets) carry binary risk. Diversified pipelines (10+ assets across modalities) deliver smoother revenue replacement.

### Pipeline Valuation

- **Risk-adjusted NPV (rNPV)** is the standard pharma-pipeline valuation framework
- Each asset: peak sales × probability of approval × discount factor − development cost
- Sum across pipeline for "pipeline NAV"
- **Sanity check**: market cap minus current-product DCF = implied pipeline value; compare to rNPV-derived pipeline NAV

## The PBM / Formulary Layer (the unique complexity)

US drug pricing has a **uniquely complex intermediation structure** that breaks the simple supply-demand model:

```
Manufacturer (sets list price)
    ↓ rebate (negotiated)
Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM)
    ↓ pricing tier / formulary placement
Insurance Plan (employer or government)
    ↓ co-pay
Patient
```

### PBM Economics — the rebate spread and the gross-to-net bubble

- **Top 3 US PBMs** (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) control ~80% of prescription volume, covering roughly half the US population
- PBMs negotiate **rebates** from manufacturers in exchange for formulary placement
- PBMs **retain a portion of the rebate** (the spread between manufacturer rebate and what's passed to plan) — they profit from the same high list prices they appear to negotiate down ([[foundation/sources/kolchinsky-great-american-drug-deal|Kolchinsky TBSC-5]])
- **Why low-list entrants are structurally resisted**: a drug launched at a low list price with no rebate delivers the same net cost but destroys the PBM's spread income — so formulary preference flows to high-list/high-rebate products. This is the engine of the **gross-to-net bubble**: list prices inflate while net prices stay flat or fall
- **The patient is the funding leak**: cost-sharing is keyed to *list*, not net — a 20% co-pay on a $10,000-list drug netting $6,000 makes the patient cover a third ($2,000) of the drug's real cost (Kolchinsky's worked example)
- **Insulin, the canonical case** (USC Schaeffer, 32 products, 2014-2018): net system spend barely changed while list prices soared; the intermediary share of each insulin dollar rose **30% → 53%**, manufacturers' share falling
- **Scale check** (Kolchinsky's own): PBM profits + excess admin ≈ $23B, <7% of $338B 2017 retail drug spend — material rent, not the whole affordability problem. Bias note: Kolchinsky (RA Capital, biotech long fund) systematically shifts blame toward PBMs/insurers; mechanics corroborated, blame allocation contested

### Why This Matters for Pharma

**A PBM that retains rebates has a financial incentive to favor higher-list-price drugs** with larger absolute rebate dollars over lower-list-price drugs with similar net price. This creates the **"list price inflation"** dynamic that's been a chronic political issue: list prices rise faster than CPI even as net prices (after rebates) stay stable.

**Implication for pharma valuation**:
- Headline list price growth ≠ revenue growth (much absorbed by rebate growth)
- **Net-revenue-per-prescription** is the operating metric, not list-price-per-prescription
- Companies disclosing "gross-to-net" deduction provide cleaner economics than companies hiding the rebate trail

### Formulary Placement Tools

- **Preferred tier**: lowest patient co-pay; drives volume
- **Non-preferred tier**: higher co-pay; reduces volume
- **Excluded**: not covered; near-zero volume
- **Step therapy**: must fail cheaper drug before insurer covers expensive drug
- **Prior authorization**: clinician must justify; volume-suppressive

### PBM-Specific Risk

- **PBM consolidation pricing power**: top-3 can extract larger rebates from manufacturers; squeezes margins
- **Direct-to-consumer pricing**: some manufacturers pursuing transparent direct pricing (Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drug Co.) — bypasses PBM but works only for select products
- **Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) negotiation**: government Medicare negotiation 2026-2030 introduces a new pricing channel (lower than commercial; typical 30-50% off net price for negotiated drugs) — political/regulatory risk vector

## Pharma-Specific Moat Indicators (financial-statement diagnostics)

Beyond standard moat indicators (ROE >15%, stable margins, high FCF), look for:

### Pipeline Indicators

- **R&D / Revenue ratio**: 15-20% typical for big pharma; below = under-investing in pipeline
- **Late-stage pipeline count**: # of Phase III candidates / current revenue exposure to LOE
- **Pipeline-to-LOE-revenue replacement ratio**: are new approvals annualizing >LOE losses?

### Pricing Power

- **Price increases (post-rebate net pricing)**: 2-4% annual is healthy; 0-1% suggests price compression
- **Geographic mix**: US > EU > Japan in pricing realization (US is 40-50% of branded global revenue)
- **Therapeutic area mix**: oncology > rare disease > neuro > cardio in pricing realization

### Capital Allocation

- **Buyback discipline**: are buybacks happening when stock is below intrinsic value?
- **M&A pipeline refresh**: $/peak-sales ratios on acquisitions; deals at <5× peak are bargains, >10× peak are expensive
- **Dividend coverage**: pharma dividends should be covered by current-product FCF, not pipeline projections

## Honest Caveats / Failure Modes

- **CoC requirement is real**. Drug-by-drug analysis requires understanding therapeutic mechanism + clinical landscape + competitive pipeline. Without this, "pharma value investing" devolves into multiple-hunting that misses pipeline binary risks. **The trader's CoC on pharma is currently weak per this research program**; this page builds substrate, doesn't substitute for therapeutic-area depth.
- **Patent cliff timing is binary, not gradual**. A company looking cheap on trailing P/E can be facing a 50% revenue cliff in 18 months. Forward earnings + LOE schedule is the operative diagnostic.
- **Pipeline failure is recurrent**. ~50% of Phase III candidates fail. A pharma thesis built on one specific Phase III asset is binary. Diversified pipeline + ongoing M&A capability is the structural stability.
- **IRA Medicare negotiation is a permanent regime change**. 2026 saw first 10 drugs negotiated; 2027 adds 15 more; 2028 adds biologics. This is a **structural margin compression** for negotiated drugs (typically 30-50% off net price). Pharma valuation models assuming pre-IRA pricing trajectories are stale.
- **PBM consolidation could continue**. If PBM market consolidates further, manufacturer rebate-extraction increases. Watch antitrust developments.
- **Biotech vs Big Pharma is different game**. Biotech (Phase I-III development-only) is binary, venture-style. Big Pharma is durable-profitable-with-cliff-risk. **Don't confuse the two** when applying value-investing methodology.
- **Geopolitical / China risk**: increasing Chinese capability in biosimilars + small-molecule manufacturing is a long-term competitive headwind for high-margin US/EU pharma. Watch supply-chain reshoring vs cost.
- **Political risk is uniquely elevated** for pharma vs other sectors. Drug pricing is a chronic political target; bipartisan support for negotiation. Don't price valuations as if pharma were a stable utility.

## Investment-Thesis Question Template (specific to pharma)

When evaluating any pharma investment, the canonical questions:

1. **What's the LOE schedule for top 5 products?** Concentration risk = single asset >25% of revenue facing LOE in <5 years
2. **What's the pipeline-to-LOE replacement ratio?** Annual new approvals expected revenue ÷ annual LOE-cliffing revenue. Target >1.0
3. **Is the moat layer-stacked or single-source?** Single-patent moats are fragile; layer-stacked (composition + method + formulation + brand) are durable
4. **What's the gross-to-net deduction trajectory?** Rising = pricing pressure; stable = preserved net pricing
5. **What's the next 24-month catalyst?** Pipeline reads, key M&A targets, IRA negotiation outcome
6. **Why is the stock cheap?** Patent cliff fear (often correct, sometimes overdone), pipeline binary risk, sector political pressure, or actual deterioration?
7. **Where does this fit on the moat-quality / cliff-risk matrix?** High moat + low near-cliff = quality; high moat + high near-cliff = restructuring opportunity; low moat + high near-cliff = avoid
8. **What's the management capital-allocation track record?** Past M&A multiples; buyback discipline; dividend coverage
