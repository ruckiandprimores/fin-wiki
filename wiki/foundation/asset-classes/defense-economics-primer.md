# Defense Economics Primer — Monopsony, Contract Types, Backlogs, the European Cycle

> **TL;DR**: Defense is the textbook **monopsony industry** — one customer (the state) whose demand is set by budget politics, not markets, buying through regulated contracts that fix the risk/margin split in advance. The moat is regulatory + scale (decreasing-cost industry → prime oligopoly; top-5 US primes take 30% of DOD contract dollars), and revenue visibility comes from **multi-year contractual backlogs** — but backlog quality varies enormously with contract type (fixed-price development backlog destroyed >$7B at Boeing on one program). The 2024–2026 European cycle is a genuine structural demand shock (NATO 5%-by-2035; Europe ~$563B in 2025, +12.6%), currently flowing ~90–95% to legacy crewed platforms — which is exactly why the falsifier-side scenarios (attritable/software-defined systems, procurement centralisation, capacity contracts) matter: they redirect the same money away from incumbent primes. CoC-build substrate for the defense vertical; this page builds understanding, it does not validate a thesis.

## Why Defense Has Wide Moats — and Why They Differ From Pharma's

Where [[foundation/asset-classes/pharma-economics-primer|pharma's moat]] is patents + regulatory approval, defense's moat is **customer structure**:

1. **Monopsony demand with political stickiness** — one buyer per country, whose purchases are appropriated by legislatures. Programs, once started, develop constituencies (jobs, bases, national champions) that make cancellation politically costly. Demand persistence is legislated, not earned.
2. **Regulatory + classification barriers to entry** — security clearances, certification, decades-long qualification cycles, ITAR-style export controls.
3. **Decreasing-cost economics** — large fixed costs, scale economies, learning-by-doing (Hartley 2017, via [[foundation/sources/kiel-european-rearmament|Kiel Report 6]]). This structurally produces **oligopoly**: few large primes per domain, often one per domain per country in Europe.
4. **Switching costs measured in decades** — a fielded platform locks in 30–50 years of upgrades, spares, sustainment revenue (frequently higher-margin than the original sale).

The vulnerability is the mirror image: **one customer means one point of failure for demand**, and the state uses its monopsony position to regulate contractor profit directly through contract design.

## The Monopsony Customer: How Demand Is Actually Set

### The US cycle (the reference machinery — [[foundation/sources/crs-defense-primers|CRS primers]])

- Two annual legislative tracks: **authorization** (NDAA) and **appropriations**. Procurement appropriations fund capital assets (aircraft, ships, missiles) under the **full funding policy** — the entire cost of an end-item appropriated in one fiscal year. Funds stay available for obligation ~3 years (shipbuilding 5).
- Scale: FY2025 US procurement title **$167.6B**; FY2024 DOD contract obligations **$445B** (more than all other federal agencies combined), split 54% services / 46% goods.
- Investor translation: **the budget request → authorization → appropriation → obligation → revenue chain is long and observable.** Demand shocks show up in budget documents *years* before they show up in revenue — which is why defense spending cycles lead defense earnings, and both can lead or lag the stocks.

### Budget politics as the demand driver

- Demand rises on threat perception and alliance commitments, not on GDP or consumer cycles — defense is one of the few genuinely **non-correlated demand streams** in an equity book (its cycle is geopolitical).
- The current demand anchor: **NATO's 5%-of-GDP-by-2035 commitment** (June 2025; ≥3.5% core + 1.5% broader security). Commitment-implied: European NATO members spending an additional **€831B/yr by 2035** (+€336B/yr core-only; SIPRI estimates via Kiel Report 6). These are *commitment-implied projections*, not appropriated money — the distinction is the thesis's main falsifier surface.
- Monopsony cuts both ways: in drawdowns (post-Cold-War 1990s), the same political machinery compresses the industry brutally — the US 1990s consolidation ("Last Supper") created today's five-prime structure.

## Contract Economics: Where Margins Are Made and Destroyed

The contract type fixes the risk/margin split **before work begins** (FAR Part 16 spectrum, per [[foundation/sources/crs-defense-primers|CRS R48784]]):

| Type | Cost risk sits with | Margin profile | Habitat |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Firm-fixed-price (FFP)** | Contractor (100%) | Uncapped upside via cost control; uncapped downside | Mature production, commercial items |
| **Fixed-price incentive (FPIF)** | Shared via formula (e.g. 80/20) | Target profit ± share of under/overrun | Transitioning programs, low-rate initial production |
| **Cost-plus-incentive/award-fee (CPIF/CPAF)** | Government (mostly) | Fee adjusted on cost/performance | Development w/ measurable targets |
| **Cost-plus-fixed-fee (CPFF)** | Government | Fixed fee ≈ mid-single-digit % of cost; "only a minimum incentive to control costs" (FAR) | R&D, early development |

**The margin logic an investor needs:**

- **Cost-plus revenue** = low margin, near-zero risk. Stable, bond-like. Development-heavy primes (early-cycle programs) carry more of it.
- **Fixed-price *production* on mature programs** = the margin-expansion zone. The learning curve accrues to the contractor: costs fall down the curve while price is fixed — this is where multi-year production programs mint money. DOD's own guidance concedes FFP is "not in the government's best interest … on production programs until costs have become stable."
- **Fixed-price *development*** = the documented value-destruction zone. The 2010s US experiment in shifting development risk onto contractors produced the **Boeing disaster set**: KC-46A tanker **>$7B over the fixed-price contract** (Defense News); FY2024 fixed-price program losses ≈ **$5.0B in one year** (KC-46A $2.0B, T-7A $1.8B, Starliner $0.5B, VC-25B $0.4B, MQ-25 $0.3B); Starliner cumulative **~$2B**. Boeing now refuses to bid fixed-price development, and the 2025 F-47 award reportedly reverted toward cost-type structure.
- **Incentive pathologies** (empirics via [[foundation/sources/kiel-european-rearmament|Kiel Report 6]]): cost-plus weakens cost discipline; maximum competition on *complex* contracts backfires (more bids → lower award price → higher overruns → higher total cost; Carril et al. 2025); industry consolidation causally shifts the mix toward cost-plus (Carril & Duggan 2020). And under FFP, inflation risk is the contractor's — no contractual relief for unanticipated inflation absent an EPA clause (2022 DOD guidance).

**Diligence question this section generates**: what share of a prime's backlog is fixed-price *development* (risk), fixed-price *production* (margin engine), and cost-type (stability)? US primes disclose this split in 10-K segment discussion; European disclosure is thinner.

## Industry Structure: Oligopoly, Tiers, and Backlog Accounting

### The prime oligopoly

- US: five primes take **30% of all DOD contract obligations** — Lockheed $50.7B (11%), RTX $24.8B, Boeing $23.2B, Northrop $18.6B, General Dynamics $15.6B (FY2024, CRS IF10600).
- Europe: the same decreasing-cost economics, but fragmented **by country** — each state protects a national champion, producing the Kiel fragmentation table: **14 MBT models vs 1 US, 23 howitzer variants vs 2, 24 torpedo types vs 3, 16 submarine types vs 4**. Low volumes, high unit costs — and simultaneously, **protected franchises**. Europe's inefficiency is the incumbents' revenue structure.

### The tiered supplier base

Primes sit atop a pyramid of subprimes and component suppliers (CRS IF10600). Tier-2/3 suppliers often have *better* economics than primes in a ramp (sole-source components, pricing power, capacity scarcity) but less visibility and higher single-program concentration. The prime is a systems-integration + political-access business as much as a manufacturing one.

### Backlog accounting — how order books convert to revenue, and why quality varies

- Backlog = contracted, unexecuted work. It converts to revenue over the production schedule (years — recall procurement funds obligate over 3–5 years and platforms deliver over decades). This is the source of the defense sector's **unusually high revenue visibility for a "cyclical."**
- **Backlog quality varies on at least four axes:**
  1. **Contract type** — $1B of fixed-price development backlog can be *negative-value* (Boeing booked forward losses on backlog it was contractually bound to execute). Fixed-price mature-production backlog is the good kind.
  2. **Funded vs unfunded** — US backlog splits into funded (appropriated) and unfunded (contracted but awaiting appropriation); unfunded backlog carries political risk.
  3. **Options vs firm orders** — framework agreements and options inflate headline backlog without commitment.
  4. **Escalation protection** — nominal fixed prices in an inflationary ramp transfer real-margin risk to the contractor.
- **Capacity contracts** (the Kiel-advocated model: government pays for standing production *capacity* + holds offtake options) would change backlog economics again — revenue for capacity maintained regardless of production, de-risking the contractor but capping the upside of a hot production line. Watch whether European procurement adopts this; it converts primes further toward regulated-utility economics.

## The European Cycle, 2024–2026 (dated section — will stale)

- **Spending**: Europe ~**$563B in 2025, +12.6%** — ~$100B more than 2024; >21% of the global $2.63T (IISS Military Balance 2026). Germany's defence spend "set to triple over the coming years" (Kiel PB 204). Europe already spends ~60% of the US's $920B and ~3× Russia.
- **Where it's going**: overwhelmingly to legacy platforms — **~95% of Germany's €100B special fund** committed to uses other than autonomous systems/data centers/satellites; ~90% to traditional crewed systems + ammunition/comms; forward appropriations >80% legacy, autonomous systems at 1%; German defence R&D stagnant at **~2% of budget** (~1/5 the US share). Near-term, this is **directly confirming for incumbent-prime revenue** — the money is flowing to exactly the companies in the [[investment/instruments/defense-instruments|instrument analysis]].
- **Capacity constraints are real and binding**: Russia out-produces all of Europe ~4× in artillery shells; IRIS-T SLM air-defence systems produced in single digits per year; Europe managed 8 orbital launches in 2025 vs ~200 US (Kiel PB 204). Constraint = pricing power for incumbents near-term; also = the opening for new entrants and reform pressure.
- **National-champion politics**: "buy-European" preferences have weak welfare evidence (Bombardini et al. 2024 on Buy American, via Kiel Report 6) but strong political momentum — a tailwind for European primes' order flow regardless of efficiency.
- **The reform agenda in play** (Kiel + Draghi direction): centralised European procurement (Italian evidence: ~28% direct savings from centralisation), capacity contracts, R&D share to 10%, open innovation calls, Ukraine industrial integration. Every element redistributes spending away from the current legacy-platform beneficiaries. Adoption is uncertain and slow — but it is the *structural* form of the thesis's falsifier.

## What to Watch / Falsifier-Relevant Structure

What would make rearmament **NOT** flow to incumbent primes (the [[investment/theses/defense-rearmament|thesis]]'s falsifier section, given structural form):

1. **Attritable / software-defined disruption.** The Ukraine template: cheap, mass-producible autonomous systems (Flamingo FP-5 cruise missile at <~$1M/unit; drones inflicting a high share of battlefield damage on traditional armour — Kiel PB 204). If the marginal defence euro shifts from exquisite crewed platforms to attritable systems, the winners are new-entrant defence-tech firms (Anduril-type) and electronics/software suppliers, not MBT/howitzer primes. **Not-yet-ingested falsifier-side source: Christian Brose, *The Kill Chain* — flagged for future ingest as the strongest articulation of this scenario.**
2. **Procurement reform favoring new entrants.** Centralised EU procurement + open/bottom-up innovation calls + SBIR-style small-firm contests (Kiel Report 6's evidence base: open calls causally increase technology adoption + VC follow-on; top-down calls mostly produce incumbent lock-in). Watch: European Defence Agency/EDIP institutional changes, any DARPA-analog agency with real budget.
3. **Contract-regime migration.** Capacity contracts (utility-ization of margins), or a renewed fixed-price-development push (margin destruction — the Boeing case is the base rate). Also US FAR overhaul via E.O. 14275 (2025) — CRS flags possible "reduced use of fixed-price contracts"; margin-structure drift in either direction.
4. **Budget walk-back.** The 5%-by-2035 numbers are commitments, not appropriations. Watch annual appropriations vs commitment path — the gap is the honest measure of trend durability (this is the thesis's falsifier #1 in operational form).
5. **R&D-share reallocation.** If Germany moves R&D from 2% toward 10% of the defence budget (Kiel's principle #1), €10–15B/yr shifts from platform procurement to technology programs — different beneficiary set.
6. **Backlog-quality erosion.** Cancellations/push-outs (thesis falsifier #2) — but also watch *composition*: rising unfunded/option share, or fixed-price development creeping back into backlog.

## Honest Caveats / Failure Modes

- **Valuation is not covered here.** This page is industry structure; the thesis carries the valuation read (EU pure-plays at 30–40× as of mid-2026, per [[investment/instruments/defense-instruments|the analysis]]). Structure says nothing about whether a re-rating is earned.
- **The monopsony can turn.** Every structural argument above ran in reverse in the 1990s. Peace scenarios (a Russia-Ukraine settlement with credible demilitarization) compress the sector regardless of moats.
- **CRS mechanics are US-specific**; European procurement law differs by country and is in flux — the *economics* transfer, the institutional detail doesn't. EU-side institutional depth is a CoC gap.
- **Kiel material is policy advocacy** (see [[foundation/sources/kiel-european-rearmament|source page]], Honest scope) — its measured facts are separable from its recommendations, but its framing has a direction.
- **Sustainment economics under-covered here**: aftermarket/spares/upgrade revenue (often the highest-margin stream, decades-long) deserves its own treatment — open question below.
