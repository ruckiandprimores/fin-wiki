# European Rearmament Economics — Kiel Report 6 + Kiel Policy Brief 204

> **TL;DR**: Two companion Kiel Institute publications that together quantify the economics of Europe's rearmament. The policy brief supplies the **fragmentation table** (Europe fields **14 main-battle-tank models vs 1 in the US, 23 howitzer variants vs 2, 16 submarine types vs 4**) and the spending frame (European defence spending ~USD 550B/yr per IISS Military Balance 2026 — second-largest in the world, ~60% of the US's $920B, yet "a fraction of the capabilities"); the Carril report supplies the procurement-mechanism economics (fixed-price + auctions for simple goods, cost-plus + negotiation for complex; capacity contracts for surge; centralisation savings). **Investor payload**: defence is a decreasing-cost industry — fragmentation along national lines is the structural cause of high unit costs AND the moat protecting national champions; the entire Kiel reform agenda (centralised EU procurement, R&D share 2%→10%, capacity contracts, autonomous systems) is, if adopted, a **redistribution of the spending surge away from legacy-platform primes**. First ingested source under the defense vertical.

## Key findings

### 1. The fragmentation economics (KPB 204, Table 1 — source: Chinn et al. 2026 / McKinsey, "European Defense by the Numbers")

| Weapons system | United States | Europe |
|---|---:|---:|
| Main battle tanks | 1 | **14** |
| 152/155mm howitzers | 2 | **23** |
| Torpedoes | 3 | **24** |
| Armored infantry fighting vehicles | 3 | **21** |
| Submarines | 4 | **16** |
| Anti-ship missiles | 3 | 8 |
| Long-range air defence systems | 4 | 8 |
| Air-to-air missiles | 5 | 15 |
| Attack helicopters | 2 | 5 |
| Tactical combat aircraft | 9 | 15 |

The brief's causal chain: "fragmentation of European defence planning and procurement along national lines, the artisanal nature of national defence production **and the lobbying power of national champions** condemns Europe to low volumes, high unit costs, limited interchangeability, and increasingly also technological backwardness." Carril adds the industrial-organisation frame (citing Hartley 2017): defence is a **decreasing-cost industry** — large fixed costs, scale economies, learning-by-doing — so market structure tends to oligopoly, and fragmenting demand across national buyers yields "smaller production scales, higher costs, and a more fragile industrial base."

### 2. The spending trajectory

- **European defence spending ~USD 550B/yr** (KPB 204, citing IISS Military Balance 2026); IISS's own release puts 2025 Europe at **almost USD 563B, +12.6%** (~USD 100B more than 2024) — see the IISS line in Sources. Second-largest bloc in the world, ahead of China.
- Europe exceeds China's defence budget by **~$150–200B/yr** (at market FX, assuming actual Chinese spend is 1/3 above official) and is **~3× Russia's**.
- Europe already spends **~60% of the US total (USD 920B, 2025)**; if ~60% of US spending is allocated to non-NATO commitments, "Europe already outspends the U.S. for NATO defence both in absolute terms and relative to GDP" (KPB 204).
- **NATO 5%-of-GDP by 2035** (agreed June 2025): ≥3.5% core defence + 1.5% broader security. Meeting it implies European NATO members collectively spending an **additional €831B/yr by 2035** (SIPRI: Tian, Scarazzato & Guiberteau Ricard 2025); core-defence-only, **+€336B/yr** (Carril, Report 6 fn.1). Baseline since 2000: Germany averaged 1.3% of GDP, France 2%, Italy 1.5%, Spain 1.4%, UK 2.3% (SIPRI).
- **Germany**: defence spending "set to triple over the coming years" (KPB 204, from federal financial planning).

### 3. Where the money actually goes (KPB 204 — German budget-line analysis)

- **~95% of the Bundeswehr's €100B Special Fund** went to uses other than autonomous systems, data centers, and satellites; **close to 90% finances traditional crewed systems** (tanks, aircraft) plus associated ammunition/communications. Only **~4%** for autonomous systems, data centers, or satellites.
- Forward appropriations passed by the Bundestag: **>80% earmarked for legacy systems; autonomous systems stuck at 1%** (≤5% even counting air defence as "defence tech").
- German defence **R&D share stagnant at ~2%** of the defence budget — roughly **1/5 the US share**; US military R&D ~**$100B/yr** vs "a couple of billion" in Germany. Kiel's recommendation: raise to ≥10% (€10–15B/yr).
- **Capacity gaps**: Russia currently produces **~4× as many artillery shells annually** as Europe despite Europe's far larger spend; IRIS-T SLM air-defence production "stuck in single-digit numbers per year"; Europe managed **8 orbital launches in 2025 vs ~200 US**. Ukraine's Flamingo FP-5 cruise missile (announced production of 200 units, priced **under ~$1M/unit**) is cited as the template of cheap mass-producible systems Europe lacks.

### 4. Procurement-mechanism economics (Carril, Report 6 — the "what works" payload)

- **One size does not fit all.** Competitive auctions + **fixed-price** contracts work best for simple, standardised goods (ammunition, low-tech drones). Complex acquisitions with non-contractible quality are better served by restricted competition, direct negotiation, and/or **cost-reimbursement** contracts.
- **Competition is not unambiguously good**: Carril, Gonzalez-Lira & Walker (2025, AER forthcoming) find US contract publicity increases bids ~60% and cuts award prices ~6% on average — but raises cost overruns and delays on complex contracts, where total costs end up *higher*. Mandated competition for everything is suboptimal.
- **Consolidation → cost-plus**: Carril & Duggan (2020) show 1986–2001 US defence-contractor consolidation causally increased use of cost-plus contracts (a margin-structure consequence of oligopoly).
- **Capacity contracts** (from the COVID vaccine playbook — Castillo et al. 2021: social value of capacity $600–1,000/course vs $6–40/course actually paid): governments should pay for *capacity*, not delivered units — capacity build-up payment + production offtake option. KPB 204 elevates this to principle #2 for Germany.
- **Innovation procurement**: DARPA-style autonomous agencies (flexible hiring, bottom-up project selection, active management) outperform rigid top-down systems; buyer human capital is causally important (Decarolis et al. 2021 — programme-manager deaths reduce patent output); bottom-up/open calls beat top-down solicitations (Howell et al. 2025); push (R&D funding) + pull (purchase commitments) combined.
- **Centralisation**: the case for a single European defence procurement market is "very strong" — Italian evidence puts direct centralisation savings **~28%** (Bandiera, Prat & Valletti 2009). Explicit buy-European preferences are the one policy with discouraging evidence ("Buy American" achieved "modest employment gains at high and increasing costs" — Bombardini et al. 2024).

### 5. Who captures the surge (synthesis — investor-relevant)

- **Near term, the money is flowing to incumbent legacy primes**: the German budget-line data (≈90–95% crewed platforms + ammo) is direct evidence that the current spending wave is a legacy-platform revenue event, not a defence-tech one.
- **The fragmentation that Kiel decries is the incumbents' moat**: national-champion lobbying sustains 14 tank models, i.e. 14 protected franchises at low volume/high unit cost. High unit costs are the *symptom*; the *income statement* of that symptom sits at the national champions.
- **The entire Kiel reform agenda is a redistribution scenario**: centralised EU procurement (fewer platforms, more head-to-head competition among incumbents), capacity contracts (revenue model shifts from unit deliveries to paid-but-idle capacity), R&D share 2%→10% (reallocates from platforms to tech), open innovation calls + small-firm contests (new entrants), Ukraine-style cheap autonomous mass (attritable systems vs exquisite platforms). Each element, if adopted, moves money away from the current beneficiaries. **How much of this agenda gets adopted is the key uncertainty the thesis's falsifier section should track.**

## Why it matters / honest scope

- This is the wiki's first quantified foundation for the defense vertical: fragmentation numbers, spending trajectory, and the procurement mechanics that determine which industrial structures monetise the surge.
- **Honest scope — think-tank policy advocacy, not neutral measurement.** Both documents are advocacy: Carril's report is a literature synthesis arguing for procurement reform; KPB 204 is co-authored by the Kiel Institute's *president* and argues for Eurobonds-for-defence (a Hildebrand–Rey–Schularick proposal, KPB 199: 1% of GDP in 10-year bonds annually for 10 years ≈ **€1.8T cumulative 2026–2035** for a 16-country "Team Europe"). The *measured facts* (budget-line breakdowns, fragmentation counts, spending levels) are separable from the *recommendations* (centralisation, capacity contracts, R&D targets) and from the *forecasts* (spending set to triple; +€831B/yr by 2035 is a commitment-implied projection, not a fact).
- The fragmentation table is second-hand (Chinn et al. 2026 — McKinsey); counts depend on how "model/variant/type" is defined. Direction and order of magnitude are robust; exact counts are definitional.
- Carril himself flags the field's weakness: defence-procurement economics is data-starved (source-selection-sensitive data withheld) and outcome measurement ("capabilities") is unresolved — so the mechanism findings rest on a thin empirical base, much of it extrapolated from non-defence procurement and the vaccine episode.
