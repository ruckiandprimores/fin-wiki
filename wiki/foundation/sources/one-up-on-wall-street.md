# One Up on Wall Street — Peter Lynch

> **TL;DR**: The amateur stock-picker's manual. Lynch's two big claims: (1) ordinary investors can beat Wall Street by paying attention to the businesses around them, and (2) stocks behave categorically — you must classify a company as slow grower / stalwart / fast grower / cyclical / turnaround / asset play before you can decide what to do with it.

## Why This Book Matters

Lynch ran Fidelity Magellan from 1977 to 1990 — turning $18M into $14B with a ~29% annualized return, the best mutual-fund record of the era. He picked stocks by looking at companies, not models. His framework is the qualitative complement to [[foundation/sources/the-intelligent-investor|Graham's quantitative framework]]:

| Graham (defensive) | Lynch (active) |
|--------------------|----------------|
| Buy when quantitative criteria pass | Buy when you understand the business and its category |
| Margin of safety in price | Margin of safety in the *story* |
| Ignore the market's mood | Use the market's mood-swings as entry/exit signals |
| Defensive vs. enterprising | Six stock categories, each with different rules |

Lynch is the canonical example of **idea generation by environmental observation** — "invest in what you know." This is the mode the wiki architecture lists as Section 5's "cross-market translator" cousin: amateur observation as a feeder for systematic research.

## Core Theses

### 1. The Amateur's Edge

> "The average person is exposed to interesting local companies and products years before the professionals."

Wall Street has structural disadvantages — committee-driven decisions, career risk, must-trade-the-list rules, late-cycle institutional buying. The amateur observing The Limited's parking lot, a Dunkin' Donuts line, Volvo dependability before it becomes consensus, has a real informational lead. *(See [[foundation/market-wisdom/amateur-edge]].)*

### 2. Stocks Are Not Equivalent — Categorize First

> "Once I've established the size of the company relative to others in a particular industry, next I place it into one of six general categories: slow growers, stalwarts, fast growers, cyclicals, asset plays, and turnarounds."

You can't apply the same buy/hold/sell logic to all stocks. A great cyclical at the wrong cycle point is a disaster; a great stalwart at 50× earnings is a disaster; a fast grower whose growth slows by 5pp can crash 70% even with rising earnings. Category dictates rules. *(See [[foundation/market-wisdom/lynch-six-categories]].)*

### 3. The 13 Attributes of a Perfect Stock

A stock is *more* attractive if its underlying company has any of these traits:
- Sounds dull / has a stupid name
- Does something dull or disagreeable
- Is a spinoff
- Institutions don't own it, analysts don't follow it
- Rumored Mafia/toxic-waste involvement
- Something depressing about it (funeral homes)
- It's in a no-growth industry
- It has a niche / exclusive franchise
- People have to keep buying it (consumables)
- It's a *user* of technology, not a maker
- Insiders are buyers
- The company is buying back shares

The unifying principle: **anything that keeps Wall Street career-risk-averse capital out of the stock keeps the price low enough for an amateur to find a real edge.** *(See [[foundation/market-wisdom/perfect-stock-attributes]].)*

### 4. Stocks to Avoid

The mirror image: hot stocks in hot industries, "the next IBM/McDonald's/Disney," diworseifications (acquisitions outside core competence), whisper stocks (the great-story-with-no-earnings IPO), middleman dependency (>25% revenue from one customer), exciting names. *(See [[foundation/market-wisdom/stocks-to-avoid-lynch]].)*

### 5. Earnings, Earnings, Earnings

> "There are many theories, but to me, it always comes down to earnings and assets. Especially earnings."

Long-term, the stock price tracks earnings. Lynch's signature exhibit (DOW chart, p. 174): the price line is a noisy lagged copy of the earnings line — but mistimed entry can mean half a decade of dead money. Earnings growth × P/E re-rating drives multi-baggers; earnings collapse + P/E compression destroys them.

### 6. Cyclical Timing Is Different

The conventional valuation rule (low P/E = cheap) **inverts** for cyclicals. A cyclical with a low trailing P/E at the peak of the cycle is a sell, not a buy — the E in P/E is about to collapse. The right time to buy is when earnings look terrible, P/E looks high, and the press is pessimistic. *(See [[trading/mechanisms/cyclical-timing]].)*

### 7. Sell By Category, Not By Price

Lynch dedicates a chapter to per-category sell rules:
- Slow grower: sell at 30–50% appreciation or fundamental deterioration
- Stalwart: sell when P/E strays well above the historical band (e.g., 15 vs typical 11–12)
- Fast grower: sell when same-store sales decline OR institutions/analysts have fully piled in OR P/E ≥ 30 with 15–20% growth
- Cyclical: sell when inventories build, capacity expansion peaks, commodity prices roll over
- Turnaround: sell once it has turned around — reclassify as the new category
- Asset play: sell when the raider arrives, not before

## Crypto Adaptation Notes

The framework translates with mutation:

**Six categories — crypto mapping (hypothesis, needs validation):**
- **Slow growers** ≈ established blue-chip L1s past their growth phase (BTC arguably; ETH arguably)
- **Stalwarts** ≈ revenue-producing protocols with steady fee growth (large DEXes, lending markets)
- **Fast growers** ≈ new L1/L2 launches with verifiable on-chain TVL/usage growth
- **Cyclicals** ≈ everything in crypto — driven by BTC dominance cycles, halving cycles, alt rotation
- **Turnarounds** ≈ post-exploit DeFi protocols, post-bear-market revivals
- **Asset plays** ≈ DAOs trading below treasury value, protocols with valuable IP underearning

**Amateur edge in crypto**: arguably *stronger* than in equities — on-chain data is public, devs/users see protocol changes before token holders, niche Discord/Telegram communities precede tier-1 exchange listings.

**Earnings substitute**: crypto lacks earnings for non-revenue tokens. Substitutes worth tracking: protocol revenue, fees-to-token-holders, real economic activity (TVL not adjusted for self-deposits). For pure narrative tokens, Lynch's framework says: don't.

**Cyclical timing is the most directly applicable mechanism for the trader's day/swing horizon.** The Lynch rule "buy when earnings look terrible and P/E looks high" maps onto "buy when on-chain activity is at multi-year lows and funding is deeply negative." See [[trading/mechanisms/cyclical-timing]].

## Honest Caveats

- **The book is dated.** Specific stock examples are from 1977–1989 with millennium update. Some industries Lynch cited as great niches (newspapers, regional Bell telcos) are now value traps. The *framework* survives; specific picks don't.
- **Lynch's claim that amateurs beat professionals is partially survivorship-biased.** The amateurs who beat the market in his stories tend to be the ones who happened to also know how to read financial statements; the rest got eaten.
- **"Buy what you know" is dangerous solo.** Without the discipline of the 13 attributes + earnings check, "buy what you know" becomes "buy whatever brand you like" — which is how amateurs end up holding consumer-favorite stocks at 60× earnings.
- **"Watering the weeds"** (his term for averaging up on winners and cutting losers) directly contradicts mean-reversion / value-investing instincts. He's right for fast growers; he's wrong for cyclicals at peaks.
