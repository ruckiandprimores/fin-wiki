# Confusión de Confusiones (Joseph de la Vega, 1688)

> **TL;DR**: The **earliest known book on the stock market** — written in 1688 about the Amsterdam exchange and Dutch East India Company (VOC) shares, in dialogue form. It already documents bulls vs bears, options ("opsies"), short-selling, coordinated manipulation, panics, and the gap between expectation and event — i.e. the full behavioral repertoire, in a lightly-regulated retail-driven market. It is the deepest historical anchor for the wiki's load-bearing prior: **crypto ≈ pre-regulation equity markets**, so pre-modern behavioral wisdom outranks 21st-century quant lit.

## Citation

Joseph (José/Josseph) Penso de la Vega, *Confusión de Confusiones* (Amsterdam, 1688). Public domain; widely available in the Fridson/Wiley *Confusion de Confusiones & Extraordinary Popular Delusions* edition and the Hermann Kellenbenz translation.

## Position in lineage

The temporal root of the wiki's behavioral cluster — older than [[lefevre-reminiscences|Lefèvre/Livermore]] (1923), [[kindleberger-manias-panics-crashes|Kindleberger]], and [[wyckoff-method|Wyckoff]]. That a 1688 market shows the *same* mechanics crypto shows in 2026 is the strongest possible evidence for [[../market-structure/crypto-carrier-features|crypto-carrier-features]] / worldview prior #1: these are **structural features of retail-driven, lightly-regulated markets**, not era-specific artifacts.

- **Axis 1 — Economic mechanism**: behavioral exploitation + manipulation-aware (coordinated rings, panics) + the options/short-selling toolkit.
- **Axis 2 — Driver**: cognitive bias + herd/coordination ("a small group becomes an army") + information asymmetry.
- **Axis 3 — Crypto applicability**: **direct** — the behavioral patterns transfer verbatim to crypto; the 1688 Amsterdam exchange is structurally closer to crypto than to modern regulated equities.

## Key content

**The four principles of speculation (verbatim):**
1. *"Never give anyone the advice to buy or sell shares"* — well-intentioned guidance backfires when judgment falters.
2. *"Take every gain without showing remorse about missed profits"* — secure available gains; don't chase the last tick.
3. *"Profits on the exchange are the treasures of goblins"* — they shift unpredictably between valuable and worthless.
4. *"Whoever wishes to win in this game must have patience and money"* — values fluctuate constantly and rumors lack foundation; emotional resilience is the edge.

**Documented market practices (in 1688):** bulls vs bears as ideological antipodes (the disciplined trader stays independent of both); options as *"the anchor of security in a storm"*; share-splitting (ducaton shares) creating arbitrage + broader participation; coordinated manipulation rings; panics and herd mimicry.

**Behavioral lessons:** three participant types (long-term holders indifferent to price, merchants timing-focused, speculators/gamblers); **markets move on opinion and news, not fundamentals**; *"the expectation of an event creates a much deeper impression upon the exchange than the event itself"* (the original statement of *buy-the-rumor*); and the speculator *"fights his own good sense, struggles against his own will."*

## Why it matters / honest scope

The "expectation precedes reality" principle is a 1688 statement of reflexivity ([[soros-alchemy|Soros]]) and of the [[../market-wisdom/bubble-cycle-stages|displacement→euphoria]] narrative cycle; the four principles are timeless position-management discipline ([[../market-wisdom/sit-tight-discipline|sit-tight]] + take-the-gain). **Honest scope:** wisdom-literature, not a backtestable mechanism — it sharpens priors and supplies the historical base-rate for "this is how retail-driven markets behave," not a signal. Use it as a *prior-calibration and graveyard-context* source, not an idea generator.
