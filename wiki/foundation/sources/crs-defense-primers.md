# US Defense Procurement Mechanics — CRS Primers (IF10599, IF10600, R48784)

> **TL;DR**: Three Congressional Research Service products that together explain how the world's largest defence customer behaves: **the budget mechanics** (procurement appropriations fund capital assets under a *full funding* policy — the entire cost of an end-item in one fiscal year; FY2025 procurement title **$167.6B**), **the customer's scale** (DOD obligated **$445B** on contracts in FY2024 — more than all other federal agencies combined; top-5 primes took **30%**), and **the contract-type economics** (the FAR Part 16 spectrum from firm-fixed-price — contractor bears all cost risk — to cost-plus-fixed-fee — government bears it; cost-reimbursement is restricted by regulation because it gives vendors "only a minimum incentive to control costs"). Payload for the defense vertical: contract type is the primary determinant of prime-contractor **margin risk** — the Boeing fixed-price-development losses are the worked disaster case CRS itself cites.

## Key findings

### 1. The budget cycle (IF10599 + R48784)

- **Procurement appropriations** fund "nonconstruction-related investment costs — the costs to acquire capital assets, such as an F-35 Joint Strike Fighter aircraft or a Virginia-class submarine."
- **Full funding policy**: Congress appropriates "the entire procurement cost of end-items … in one fiscal year." Consequence: end-item purchases arrive as discrete annual appropriation events, not smooth flows — quantity decisions are re-litigated politically every year.
- Procurement funds typically remain available for obligation **3 years** (Navy shipbuilding: **5 years**) — the lag between appropriation, obligation, and contractor revenue recognition.
- **FY2025 procurement title: $167.6B** (P.L. 119-4). Largest accounts: Navy Shipbuilding & Conversion $33.3B, Air Force Other Procurement $31.0B, Air Force Aircraft $19.9B, Navy Aircraft $15.9B.
- The cycle runs through two separate tracks — annual **authorization** (NDAA) and **appropriations** (defense appropriations act) — CRS maintains sibling primers on each (IF10515/IF10516/IF10514; noted, not ingested).
- Statutory frame: Titles 10 & 41 U.S.C.; executive-branch implementation via the **FAR** and DOD's **DFARS** supplement; DOD also has Other Transaction Authority (OTA) outside standard FPDS-tracked contracting.

### 2. The customer's scale — monopsony in numbers (IF10600 + R48784)

- FY2024 total US federal procurement: **>$750B**; DOD ≈ **60%** of it.
- **DOD FY2024 contract obligations: $445B** — "exceeding all other federal agencies combined." Split **54% services / 46% goods**.
- **Top-5 concentration**: Lockheed Martin $50.7B (11%), RTX $24.8B (6%), Boeing $23.2B (5%), Northrop Grumman $18.6B (4%), General Dynamics $15.6B (4%) — **30% of all DOD contract obligations to five firms**.
- Tiered structure: primes "subcontract to other companies that serve as subprime contractors" — the prime's income statement sits atop a supplier pyramid.

### 3. Contract-type economics — the FAR Part 16 spectrum (R48784)

- Contract types sit on a spectrum "from **firm-fixed-price**, in which the contractor has full responsibility for the performance costs and resulting profit (or loss), to **cost-plus-fixed-fee**, in which the contractor has minimal responsibility for the performance costs and the negotiated fee (profit) is fixed" (FAR §16.101(b)).
- **Fixed-price** (6 subtypes incl. FFP, fixed-price w/ economic price adjustment, fixed-price-incentive): FAR calls them "ordinarily in the government's interest" — "maximum incentive for the contractor to control costs." Selected "when the risk involved is minimal or can be predicted with an acceptable degree of certainty." Key DOD guidance: "it is … **not in the government's best interest to use FFP contracts on production programs until costs have become stable**" — i.e. mature production runs to FFP, development shouldn't.
- **Fixed-price-incentive (FPIF)**: sharing formulas (worked example: $1B low-rate-initial-production target, 20% share line — contractor keeps 20% of underruns, eats 20% of overruns against target profit). DOD's stated sweet spot for transitioning programs.
- **Cost-reimbursement** (5 subtypes incl. cost, cost-sharing, CPIF, CPAF, CPFF): permitted only when requirements or costs can't be estimated well enough for fixed-price; **prohibited for commercial goods/services**; needs written approval one level above the contracting officer. FAR: cost-plus-fixed-fee gives vendors "**only a minimum incentive to control costs**" — the canonical incentive pathology; the government eats overrun risk. Typical habitat: R&D and technical services.
- **Incentive and indefinite-delivery (IDIQ)** categories round out the menu; time-and-materials allowed for DOD only ≤$150,000.
- Under FFP, DOD had "no authority to provide contractual relief for unanticipated inflation" (2022 guidance) — fixed-price backlog is nominal-price backlog; inflation risk sits with the contractor unless an EPA clause exists.

### 4. Incentive pathologies + the fixed-price development disasters (R48784 "Issues for Congress" + press)

- The symmetric pathology pair: **cost-plus** → weak cost control (government bears overruns); **fixed-price on immature/complex work** → contractor losses, and the government still "may assume schedule or performance risk if it is unable to obtain what is needed."
- **Worked disaster case — Boeing** (CRS names it; magnitudes from cited press): the KC-46A tanker fixed-price development contract has cost Boeing **more than $7B over the contract price** (Defense News); 2024 full-year fixed-price program losses ≈ **$5.0B** (KC-46A $2.0B, T-7A $1.8B, Commercial Crew/Starliner $0.5B, VC-25B $0.4B, MQ-25 $0.3B — Breaking Defense/Defense One); cumulative Starliner losses **~$2B** (SpacePolicyOnline). Boeing management has said it will **no longer bid fixed-price development contracts**, and the 2025 F-47 fighter award reportedly moved away from fixed-price development (Leeham News).
- CRS flags that Congress "may consider changing the types of contracts DOD is and is not permitted to use based on the technical complexity or cost of the respective program."
- **Regime-change watch**: E.O. 14275 (Apr 2025) orders a FAR overhaul stripping non-statutory provisions — could loosen contract-type selection rules; E.O. 14240 (Mar 2025) consolidates common-goods procurement into GSA. CRS notes outcomes could include "reduced use of fixed-price contracts." Both are live regulatory-drift vectors for prime margin structure.

## Why it matters / honest scope

- For an investor in defence primes, **contract-type mix is the margin-quality variable**: cost-plus revenue = low-risk, capped-margin, stable; fixed-price *production* on mature programs = the margin-expansion zone (learning curve accrues to the contractor); fixed-price *development* = the documented value-destruction zone. Backlog composition by contract type is therefore not disclosure trivia — it prices risk.
- **Honest scope**: CRS is descriptive and US-only. European procurement (the thesis's main arena) runs on different legal machinery — the Kiel source covers that side; the FAR framework transfers as *economics* (risk-allocation logic is universal) but not as *institutional fact*. The Boeing loss magnitudes are from press/earnings coverage, not CRS itself (CRS cites the earnings calls and the Defense News $7B figure). IF10599/IF10600 were read via everycrsreport mirrors of periodically-updated products — figures are FY2024/FY2025 vintage and will age.
