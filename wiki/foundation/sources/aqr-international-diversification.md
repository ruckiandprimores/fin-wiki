# AQR — International Diversification: Still Not Crazy after All These Years

> **TL;DR**: Asness, Ilmanen & Villalon (AQR), *Journal of Portfolio Management* 49(6), June 2023 — fetched
> and read in full (all 10 pages). Core result: **the entire +4.6%/yr US-vs-EAFE outperformance since
> 1990 collapses to a statistically insignificant +1.2%/yr once you control for the CAPE re-rating** (US
> CAPE went from ~half of EAFE's in 1990 to ~1.5× EAFE's by end-2022). Second load-bearing result: **at
> short horizons (months/quarters) global and local portfolios crash together, but from ~2 years out,
> globally diversified portfolios recover meaningfully while single-country worst-case portfolios do
> not** (Exhibit 1, 22 countries, 1950–2022) — the mechanism the wiki's "5–10 year hold" framing rests on.
> Honest counterpoint carried throughout: US equities actually **underperformed EAFE in 3 of the 5
> decades before the 1990 sample began**, and the authors explicitly do not claim mean reversion is
> guaranteed to repeat.

## The paper's five-point structure

The authors state upfront they'll argue **for** diversification on points 1, 3, 4, 5 and concede the
honest **against** case on point 2 before countering it.

### Point 1 — Theory: market-clearing logic

Diversification is the only "market-clearing"/"macro-consistent" portfolio — if every country's investors
believed their home market would outperform, that's mathematically impossible in aggregate. The resulting
case *against* diversification would have to rest on **"US exceptionalism rather than home bias"** — a
structural claim that the US is simply the best market, not a behavioral quirk of investors elsewhere.
The paper notes some long-run evidence superficially supports this: **Dimson, Marsh and Staunton's (2022)
annual dataset estimates US markets outperformed non-US ones by about 2%/year from 1900 through 2021**
(a 122-year edge) — see the footnote-6 discussion below for the caveat AQR immediately attaches to this number.

### Point 2 — The honest concession: crashes correlate, but decades don't

**Conceded directly**: "At shorter horizons (e.g., months and quarters), worst cases for individual
countries are similar to worst cases for globally diversified portfolios. Markets become more correlated
in periods of great stress, so having your equity allocation spread out across multiple countries usually
doesn't save you from a big crash." This is **not** a red herring the authors dismiss — they state it
plainly as true.

**But then the reframe**: extended bear markets, not short crashes, are what actually derail investors'
long-term wealth goals. Exhibit 1 (methodology from Asness, Israelov & Liew 2011, updated through 2022;
22 countries, monthly data, Jan 1950–Dec 2022) plots average worst-return paths by horizon:
- At short horizons, the "average global return during worst local return events" line tracks close to
  the worst-local and worst-global lines (all deeply negative, roughly −20% to −60%+ real).
- From roughly 2 years (24 months) out, the lines diverge sharply: **the globally diversified
  portfolio's return during the worst-local-return periods recovers toward ~0% and eventually positive
  territory by ~7–10 years, while the worst-local-return line stays deeply negative (roughly −60% to
  −70% real) even at 10 years.**
- Mechanism: countries' *cyclical and secular* economic/market paths diverge even when their *crashes*
  correlate — the paper's example is Japan's pre-1990 boom vs its post-1990 multi-decade bust, a path no
  other major market matched over the same window.

**Wiki-relevance**: this is the direct empirical justification for framing the ex-US leg as a 5–10 year
structural hold rather than a tactical or crash-hedge position — the paper's own data shows the
diversification payoff is specifically a multi-year phenomenon, closely aligned with the horizon already
stated in the ex-US thesis.

### Point 3 — The US's multi-decade victory is mostly a valuation story

**The single most load-bearing number in the paper**: since 1990, the US outperformed the MSCI EAFE Index
(currency-hedged) by **+4.6%/year**. Over the same period, the US Shiller CAPE ratio went from **about
half of EAFE's (1990) to 1.5× EAFE's (end-2022)** — a tripling of relative valuation. Regressing returns
against contemporaneous valuation changes (isolating the "what would the return have been if the asset
hadn't gotten cheaper/more expensive" intercept) **collapses the +4.6%/yr edge to a statistically
insignificant +1.2%/yr.**

Robustness check offered: the same test on the more recent 2008–2022 window shows US-vs-EAFE
outperformance of +4.7%/yr headline, again collapsing to **+1.2%/yr (again statistically insignificant)**
once valuation changes are controlled for — even though the US was ahead in 12 of those 15 years.

**Explicit honest counterpoint (footnote 16 + body text)**: "US equities actually underperformed EAFE in
three of the past five decades (1970s, 1980s, 2000s), including the two decades just before our sample
period began." From January 1970 to end-February 2023 (the longest window given), US equities
outperformed EAFE by only **+0.8%/yr currency-hedged (+0.9%/yr unhedged)** — a far smaller edge than the
1990-onward number, because it captures decades where the US actually lost.

**Footnote 6 — the "US exceptionalism as luck/selection" argument** (the specific citation trail for the
survivorship-style caveat): *"This 122-year edge came largely through higher dividends-per-share growth,
though we'll soon see that the edge in recent decades came mainly from changing relative valuations. The
historical US exceptionalism needs not have been all in the market's control—or to have been predictable
and repeatable. It's entirely possible, for instance, that the big underlying contributors could have been
things outside the direct result of corporate skill, which we might call 'luck' (e.g., no revolutions over
the period, no hyperinflation, no wars on home soil)."* Cited source: **Dimson, Marsh, and Staunton
(2021), "American Exceptionalism: The Long-Term Evidence," The Journal of Portfolio Management 47(7):
14–26** — a standalone DMS paper making the survivorship/selection argument explicitly, not independently
fetched for this ingest (flagged REPORTED via AQR's citation, not verified firsthand).

### Point 4 — What investors should infer from today's valuations

Exhibit 2 (Jan 1980–Feb 2023): the US/EAFE CAPE ratio rose through the 2010s to a historic high of
**1.8× in 2020–2021**, fell in 2022, but "remains extremely wide." The paper's logic is plain economic
reasoning, not a timing call: countries selling at lower valuations relative to fundamentals should have
a higher long-run expected return — this doesn't require CAPE to mean-revert on any particular schedule,
just that the "carry" of a lower entry multiple is itself sufficient justification. Explicit hedge: **"we
don't know what the catalyst will be or when"** — no market-timing claim is made, and the paper notes the
2010s were a period where contrarian/valuation-based country allocation and stock selection alike
underperformed for a very long time, i.e., mean reversion "at extreme levels" is a tendency, not a
certainty on any fixed horizon.

### Point 5 — Active investors get a second diversification benefit: factors, not just markets

Exhibit 3 (24 developed equity markets, Jan 2003–Dec 2022, monthly): median cross-country correlation for
the raw **market** factor is **~0.75** (high — consistent with Point 2's crash-correlation finding), but
median cross-country correlation drops to **~0.26 for the Value factor** and **~0.42 for Momentum** (long–short
factor returns, not raw market exposure). Long–short country-factor strategies diversify much better
internationally than market-cap-weighted country exposure does, because they strip out the shared
market-directional component.

### Conclusion — behavioral, not analytical, is the hard part

The paper's own framing of the real difficulty: diversified portfolios always look "full of mistakes" ex
post (you'll always have underperforming positions somewhere), and organizations tend to reward whoever
happens to have been overweight the winning market, creating a principal–agent pressure against
maintaining unpopular diversified positions — "no pain, no premium." The stated bottom line: international
diversification is still worth it even after 30 years of not paying off for US investors, because the
US's edge over that window mostly reflects valuation richening rather than a repeatable structural
advantage, and if anything, historically wide relative valuations point the other way.

## Honest caveats (author-stated + noted here)

- **US home bias is "less of a mistake than it looks"** for US investors specifically (footnote 4): US
  equities are already ~half of world market cap, and ~30% of S&P 500 firms' revenue comes from abroad —
  so a "US-only" US investor already carries meaningful indirect international exposure via corporate
  revenue mix. This is explicitly NOT true to the same degree for non-US investors.
- **Currency and industry-mix effects are set aside**, not because they're unimportant, but because the
  paper's central argument (valuation-driven relative performance) is cleaner isolated from those two
  confounds (footnote 3).
- **No claim that mean reversion is guaranteed or dated** — the entire Point 4 argument is conditional
  ("if anything... may point to prospective underperformance"), not a forecast with a timeline.
- **The regression-intercept method has limits** — the paper itself flags (footnote 13) that this
  valuation-adjustment technique works "pretty well" for stocks/bonds but is noisier for higher-turnover
  strategies, where "wedges" between returns and valuation changes creep in.
