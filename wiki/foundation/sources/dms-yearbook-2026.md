# UBS Global Investment Returns Yearbook 2026 — Public Summary Edition

> **TL;DR**: The 2026 edition of the DMS (Dimson/Marsh/Staunton) database — 126 years of cross-country
> asset returns (1900–2025), 35 markets. Fetched and read directly: the **19-page free public summary**
> (not the full 310-page Yearbook, which is subscriber/client-only). Headline findings for the wiki's
> ex-US work: **US equity market concentration is at its highest level in at least 100 years** (~62% of
> world equity market cap vs a ~24→36% GDP share over the same period) — a genuinely record
> concentration, not a manufactured framing; **developed markets beat emerging 8.5%/yr vs 6.9%/yr real
> since 1900**, though EM led from 1960 (10.9% vs 9.6%); **economic risk has historically mattered more
> than geopolitical risk** for forward equity returns (no measurable relationship at 1-month or 1-year
> horizons); and **diversification still works when currency-hedged**, even though it's "getting more
> challenging" (DM/EM and equity/bond correlations both rising).

## Key findings

### 1. US concentration is at a 100-year+ high, even as its GDP share has been falling since the 1940s

Figure 8 (relative GDP, end-1899 → start-2026): US GDP share rose from **24% to 36%** of the DMS-35
total. But the equity-market picture diverges sharply from GDP: **"the US market now dominates, today
accounting for around 62% of total world equity market value, despite its decline in relative GDP share
since a peak in the late 1940s. This reflects the substantial returns from US stocks and the large volume
of seasoned equity offerings and IPOs."** Section 8 states plainly: **"By end-2025, US equity market
concentration was at its highest level for at least 100 years."**

**Wiki-relevance**: this is the empirical anchor for the ex-US thesis's "concentration-reduction" framing
— the 57–71% US-book concentration the thesis cites is a portfolio-level fact sitting inside a market
that is *itself* at record concentration, not an idiosyncratic quirk of one portfolio.

### 2. The 126-year US asset-class record (Figure 12, 1900–2025)

| Asset | Nominal %/yr | Real %/yr | $1 (1900) → nominal | $1 (1900) → real |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Equities | 9.8% | 6.6% | $124,854 | $3,296 |
| Bonds | 4.6% | 1.6% | $284 | $7.5 |
| Bills | 3.4% | 0.5% | $69 | $1.8 |
| Inflation | 2.9% | — | $38 | — |

Equities were the best-performing asset class in **all 21** Yearbook countries with continuous
investment histories since 1900; bonds beat bills in every country **except Portugal**.

### 3. Developed vs emerging markets, 1900–2025 (Figure 17)

| Window | DM equities | EM equities | DM bonds | EM bonds |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 1900–2025 | **8.5%/yr** ($28,402 cumulative) | **6.9%/yr** ($4,404) | 4.4%/yr ($228) | 2.6%/yr ($26) |
| 1960–2025 | 9.6%/yr | **10.9%/yr** | — | — |

EM bonds plummeted through the 1940s but **outperformed DM bonds from 1950–2025** (also true from 1960
and 1980 start dates, though not 1970).

### 4. Economic risk beats geopolitical risk as a forward-return predictor

Using a geopolitical-risk index (Caldara & Iacoviello 2022) regressed against future world equity
returns, the Yearbook finds **no relationship, whether looking a month or a year ahead**. Of the four
largest peacetime bear markets since 1900, **three were triggered by economic factors**; the 1973–74 oil
shock was geopolitically activated but "played out as an economic crisis." WWI, WWII, and the 1973–74 Oil
Shock were 3 of the 6 worst episodes for large global equity markets since 1900 — i.e., genuine
geopolitical shocks matter, but rarely, and mostly when they cascade into an economic crisis.

### 5. Diversification is harder than it used to be, but still positive after hedging

By end-2025, alongside record US concentration, **correlations between DM and EM, and between equities
and bonds, have both risen**. Despite this, Figure 62 (32 world-index markets, 1974–2025) shows that
**when currency risk is hedged, investors in the vast majority of markets were better off investing
globally than domestically** (measured as the Sharpe-ratio uplift from going global) — the unhedged
comparison is much more mixed (negative for CHL, MEX, USA, ZAF, NLD, CHE). Separately: equities and bonds
have each lost >70% in real terms on several occasions since 1900, but a 60:40 blend has never declined
more than 50% — cross-asset diversification has a genuinely different tail profile than single-asset risk.

### 6. Currency hedging mechanics

Currency risk added **~6 percentage points on average to total portfolio risk** (equities or bonds) for
a non-local investor, though the proportional effect is larger for bond portfolios (since bonds are
lower-vol to begin with) — cited by the Yearbook as the likely reason institutional bond investors hedge
more than equity investors.

### 7. Gold — weak short-run inflation hedge, real long-run store of value

Of 28 years where inflation exceeded 3%, gold returns were **negative in 13 of them** — the short-run
inflation-hedge story is weak. But real USD gold price has risen **5.2-fold since 1900** (1.3%/yr
annualized real); in the 54 years since Bretton Woods, annualized real gold returns were higher: **4.7%
(US), 5.8% (UK), 4.3% (Switzerland)**.

### 8. Factor investing — momentum the strongest, but not immune to decade-long droughts

Momentum delivered a **7.7%/yr premium in the US** and ranked the top-performing factor in **13 of 24**
country-decades studied (1900–2025, US + UK). But **22% of country-decades saw a negative factor
premium — even measured over an entire decade** — a caution against assuming any factor premium is
reliably positive on any fixed horizon.

## What the 2026 edition specifically added (per the foreword)

New research this edition: (i) whether waves of technological innovation — explicitly including the
current AI buildout — have historically produced bubbles, and how such cycles affected long-run returns;
(ii) a deeper historical look at gold's evolving role as inflation hedge / safe haven; (iii) new work on
when geopolitical risk has materially influenced returns (feeds finding #4 above); (iv) new material on
currency hedging (feeds finding #6 above).

## Honest caveats

- **This is the free summary, not the full Yearbook.** 19 pages distilling "ten of the most important
  findings" from a 310-page report — necessarily a curated subset. Country-by-country tables, the full
  factor-premium history, and the AI-bubble/geopolitical-risk deep dives referenced in the foreword are
  NOT in the summary; only their headline conclusions are.
- **The "US exceptionalism as selection effect" argument is NOT explicitly made in this summary.**
  DMS's broader body of work (their standalone 2021 JPM paper "American Exceptionalism: The Long-Term
  Evidence" — cited by AQR, see [[foundation/sources/aqr-international-diversification]] §footnote-6, not
  independently fetched here) argues that a meaningful share of the US's long-run equity edge reflects
  historical "luck" (no revolutions, no hyperinflation, no wars fought on home soil) rather than a
  repeatable structural advantage. That specific survivorship/selection framing is **REPORTED via the AQR
  citation, not verified in this 2026 summary document** — flagged rather than merged, to avoid
  overclaiming what this particular source says.
- **Country/market definitions shift over time** (Finland, Japan, Portugal, Spain were EM by earlier
  standards) — the DM/EM long-run comparison is sensitive to classification choices the summary doesn't
  fully spell out.
- Figures are gross of investor-level fees, taxes, and (for the cross-country return series) transaction
  costs — a live-portfolio number would be lower than every headline return cited here.
