# The Great American Drug Deal / The Biotech Social Contract (Kolchinsky 2017-2020)

> **TL;DR**: A biotech fund manager's structural theory of US drug pricing. Core move: reframe branded-drug prices as **mortgage payments society makes on a permanent asset** — the drug's eventual generic form. **Genericization is the system's designed payoff, not a bug**: the patent cliff is how the contract settles. Two investor-grade payloads: (1) the sharpest freely available mechanism map of **PBM rebate-spread economics and the gross-to-net bubble** (the wiki's named pharma capability gap), and (2) a **taxonomy of contract-breaking behavior** (price-jacking, evergreening, biosimilar-stalling) usable as a red-flag screen on pharma names. Carries a known author bias: Kolchinsky runs a biotech long fund and systematically shifts blame from manufacturers to insurers/PBMs — read the PBM mechanics as strong, the blame allocation as contested.

## Citation

Peter Kolchinsky, *The Great American Drug Deal: A New Prescription for Innovative and Affordable Medicines* (Evelexa Press, 2020) — grown out of his essay series **"The Biotech Social Contract"** (Medium, 2017-2019, TBSC parts 1-9+). Kolchinsky is co-founder / Managing Partner of RA Capital Management (multi-stage biotech/healthcare fund).

## Key frameworks

### 1. The Biotech Social Contract — drug prices as mortgage payments

Society pays temporarily high branded prices in exchange for a permanent asset: once patents expire, the drug becomes a near-free public good forever ("a borrower spends 15-30 years paying off a mortgage... once the house is paid off, it is lived in rent free"). Contrast: hospitals, surgery, dialysis are "pure rent" — expensive forever. Who moves money and why: payers accept ~1.8% of GDP in branded spend (2015) because the industry converts it into the "mountain of generics" — 89% of US prescriptions are generic yet cost only ~$88B vs ~$237B for the branded 11%. Lipitor's price fell ~95% at genericization and stayed there. ([TBSC-1], [Patent-Burning Party])

**Investor implication — contractual genericization and terminal value:** the patent cliff is the *fulfillment* of the deal, not a failure mode. A pharma DCF should treat branded economics on any single asset as a wasting asset with near-zero branded terminal value at LOE; durable terminal value lives in the *platform* (pipeline replacement, M&A capability), not the molecule. A company whose valuation implies branded pricing in perpetuity is priced against the social contract — and the contract has political enforcement teeth.

### 2. What won't genericize — and the "synthetic genericization" fix

~96% of Medicare/Medicaid drug spend eventually faces generic or biosimilar competition; the residual ~2% (one-time gene/cell therapies, tiny sole-source markets) has no natural competition path — a biosimilar can't run trials when parents won't randomize a working infant gene therapy. Kolchinsky's proposal: after a fair monopoly period (~20 yrs), government contracting caps margins "modestly more than cost of production" (biodefense/BARDA precedent), or compels manufacturing tech-transfer to second/third sources. ([TBSC-2])

**Investor implication:** the non-genericizable sliver is where indefinite-monopoly pricing assumptions get made — and where regulatory-intervention risk concentrates. Gene-therapy platform valuations extrapolating monopoly pricing across "10% of 6,000 genetic disorders ≈ $200B/yr" run into the affordability wall that forces exactly this kind of intervention.

### 3. PBM economics — the rebate spread and the gross-to-net bubble (the primer's named gap)

The mechanism, from the primary essay ([TBSC-5 "False Heroes"]): manufacturers set high list prices; PBMs negotiate rebates in exchange for formulary placement and **"retain a portion of the rebates that pass through them, profiting from the same high list prices they appear to heroically negotiate down."** Three PBMs (Express Scripts, CVS/Caremark, OptumRx) control ~80% of the market covering half the US population. Who moves money and why: a manufacturer that launched at a *low list price with no rebate* would deliver the same net cost but **destroy the PBM's spread income** — so PBMs steer formulary preference toward high-list/high-rebate products, and "list prices stay high while the PBMs collect their rent." This is why low-list entrants are structurally disadvantaged, and why list prices inflate even when net prices are flat or falling — the **gross-to-net bubble**.

The patient is the funding leak: cost-sharing is keyed to *list*, not net. Kolchinsky's worked example: a 20% co-pay on a $10,000-list drug that nets the PBM $6,000 means the patient covers a third ($2,000) of the drug's real cost. Scale check (his own): PBM profits + excess admin ≈ $23B, under 7% of $338B 2017 retail drug spend — material rent, but not the whole affordability problem. Biosimilar friction case: transitioning patients to biosimilars would save the system ~$6B but cut PBM profits ~84% on those flows, so PBMs slow-walk it (via review — verify against book).

**Insulin as the canonical case (external corroboration, not from a fetched Kolchinsky essay):** USC Schaeffer (2021, 32 insulin products, 2014-2018) found net system expenditure on insulin **barely changed** while list prices soared — the intermediary share (PBMs, pharmacies, wholesalers, plans) of each insulin dollar rose from **30% (2014) to 53% (2018)**, manufacturers' share falling. List-price outrage at manufacturers largely mispriced where the money went. Kolchinsky treats insulin in the book (verify against book).

**Investor implications:** (a) for pharma names, **gross-to-net deduction trajectory is the operating diagnostic** — rising G2N spread = the manufacturer is buying formulary access with rebate dollars, i.e. pricing power is weaker than list-price optics; (b) for managed-care names (CVS, Cigna/Evernorth, UNH/Optum), the rebate spread IS an earnings driver and a standing regulatory-risk vector — rebate-reform proposals attack their P&L directly; (c) low-list disrupters (Cost Plus Drugs, direct-to-consumer) fight the channel's incentives, not just incumbents' prices — adoption will be structurally slow.

### 4. Insurance design is the access problem — not list price

Kolchinsky's reframe: US patients can't afford drugs because insurance is designed to make *sick* people pay at the point of sale — high deductibles/co-pays keyed to list price — which he calls an "institutionalized failure to insure." Roughly 50% of patients are non-adherent for cost reasons; abandonment shows up even at $1-5 co-pays. The sick subsidize the healthy: cost-sharing concentrates the system's drug bill on the users. His fix: universal coverage with minimal out-of-pocket cost on prescribed drugs; make premiums, not co-pays, carry the cost. ([TBSC-1], [TBSC-5]; Kirkus notes he simultaneously dismisses single-payer "in the fine print" — via review.)

**Investor implication:** out-of-pocket caps (IRA's $2,000 Medicare cap, insulin $35 cap) are *volume-positive* for manufacturers — they attack abandonment, not net price. Distinguish policy that squeezes net price (IRA negotiation) from policy that fixes cost-sharing (volume tailwind).

### 5. Contract-breaker taxonomy — a red-flag screen for pharma names

Kolchinsky's own taxonomy of behavior that violates the contract (each is a screenable pattern):

- **Price-jacking off-patent sole-source drugs** — Turing/Daraprim: 55-fold hike ($13.50 → $750/pill) on a 1950s off-patent drug with ~8-12k prescriptions/yr — a market too small to attract generic entrants, with REMS closed-distribution abused to deny competitors the reference samples needed for bioequivalence studies. Revenue built this way is regulatory-arbitrage rent, not innovation. ([TBSC-3])
- **Evergreening / biosimilar-stalling** — "every legal means" to delay biosimilar uptake; Kolchinsky sizes the stall cost at ~$10B/yr (<3% of drug spend). Distinct from legitimate lifecycle innovation (new formulation with real benefit, new indications). ([TBSC-1])
- **Non-innovative rebranding / price hikes** — repackaging old drugs without improvement; steep in-patent price escalation beyond launch price. ([Patent-Burning Party])
- **Legitimate behavior for contrast:** me-too/in-class competition is pro-contract — payers "play each drug off one another" for rebate concessions pre-genericization (HCV: AbbVie's inferior-convenience Viekira took <1/5 share vs Harvoni yet forced class-wide price competition worth billions). A pharma competing on rebates in a crowded class is behaving normally; one whose growth is price-jacking or stall tactics is harvesting political risk. ([TBSC-4])
- **Special case — post-generic infrastructure vacuum** (epinephrine/EpiPen): the drug is ancient, the device patented; monopoly margins funded the anaphylaxis education ecosystem, and pure generic competition funds no education. Not villainy but a structural gap; explains why some off-patent products sustain premium pricing longer than naive analysis expects. ([Epinephrine essay])

**Screen questions for any pharma holding:** What fraction of revenue growth is net-price-hike vs volume vs new products? Any sole-source off-patent products with recent large hikes? Pending biosimilar/generic entries the company is litigating or REMS-blocking? These flag both revenue quality and headline/regulatory risk.

## Why it matters / honest scope

- **Fills the named PBM gross-to-net gap** in the pharma primer with mechanism-level detail from primary-authored material, and gives the pharma vertical a reusable red-flag screen plus a terminal-value discipline (genericization-as-designed-payoff).
- **Author bias — carry it always:** Kolchinsky is a career biotech long investor (RA Capital). The book's blame allocation runs *away from* manufacturers and *toward* insurers/PBMs; Kirkus calls the shift potentially "self-serving," and Frank David (Forbes) notes it is advocacy, not academic analysis, from an acknowledged interested party. The PBM spread mechanics are well-corroborated externally (USC Schaeffer, gross-to-net literature); the implicit claim that manufacturer pricing is near-blameless is the contested part. His own number cuts against overreach: PBM rent ≈ 7% of retail drug spend — real, but not the whole story.
- **Synthesis-grade:** built from essays + reviews, not the book. Chapter-level claims and the insulin chapter are unverified against the text; items marked "(via review — verify against book)" accordingly. Pre-IRA vintage (2017-2020): the book predates Medicare negotiation, the $35 insulin cap, and the 2023-25 insulin list-price cuts — its *mechanisms* aged well (the insulin cuts happened partly because rebate reform made high-list strategies less tenable), but its *numbers* are dated.
