# Manias, Panics, and Crashes — Charles P. Kindleberger

> **TL;DR**: The canonical financial-bubble framework. Kindleberger (1910–2003) applies Hyman Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis to ~400 years of crisis history, producing the **5-stage bubble cycle** (Displacement → Boom → Euphoria → Distress → Panic/Revulsion) and operationalizing **Minsky's three-finance taxonomy** (hedge / speculative / Ponzi). Fills the **#1 priority** following Lefèvre + Wyckoff on the [[foundation/sources/lineage|lineage]] — the *bubble dynamics dimension* of the pre-regulation/behavioral cluster. Crypto cycles ARE bubble cycles; the framework is essential for regime-conditioning of strategy selection.

## Why This Source Matters For The Wiki

The pre-regulation/behavioral cluster's core was complete after Lefèvre + Wyckoff (the experiential trader's account + the analytical formalization of microstructure). What was *missing*: the **macro / credit-cycle dimension** that explains *why* manias happen at all, what their *aggregate* signature is, and what the *terminating conditions* are. Kindleberger fills this dimension.

Specifically, Kindleberger gives the wiki:

1. **A regime classification** — every market state can be categorized as one of 5 stages, with associated strategy implications.
2. **A credit-cycle diagnostic** — Minsky's three-finance taxonomy lets us classify the *aggregate* fragility of the system (not just individual asset valuations).
3. **A historical reference** — 400 years of crises with structural commonalities. Pattern-matching over multiple cycles is more reliable than analyzing one cycle in isolation.
4. **A LOLR framework** — the lender-of-last-resort debate has direct implications for crypto (which has no LOLR by design, with measurable consequences for drawdown depth).
5. **A worked example for crypto** — 2020-2022 cycle maps cleanly onto the framework, validating that crypto cycles ARE bubble cycles in the Minsky-Kindleberger sense.

For the trader's day/swing trading task: phase identification (Boom vs. Distress) is a *regime-conditioning* input that changes which mechanisms work. Trend-following works in Boom; volatility-expansion + liquidation-cascade-aftermath dominate in Distress and Panic. The framework conditions strategy selection.

## Honest Caveats

### Source-availability caveat

Unlike Lefèvre (1923) and Wyckoff (1910/1931) — both **public domain US** with full primary text ingested — Kindleberger 1978 (and successor editions) is **not in public domain** and the wiki's primary-text access is limited to **Chapter 2 only** (legitimate academic accessibility via the Miles Corak academic posting). Other chapters of the book (the historical case studies, Chs 4–13) were not ingested as primary text. The wiki synthesis draws on:
- Ch 2 verbatim (the framework chapter)
- Secondary academic summaries and modern restatements (Variant Perception, Pragmatic Capitalism, multiple writeups)
- Minsky's primary work (Levy Institute, fully accessible)
- Crypto-cycle case-study sources (CoinDesk, Decrypt, news coverage)

This is a **research-synthesis ingest**, not a primary-text ingest. Source attribution flagged accordingly.

### Framework caveats (per Section I of distillation notes)

- **Doesn't predict timing.** The framework can identify which stage a cycle is currently in, but cannot predict when distress flips to panic. UST collapsed in 72 hours; FTX in 72 hours; but the broader bear market rolled over 12 months. The framework conditions strategy selection, not entry timing.
- **Doesn't predict magnitude.** "Panic produces sharp price decline" — but -30%, -60%, or -90%? Calibration is per-asset-class. Crypto's -77% in 2022 was at the high end; equities -56% in 2008 was lower (with LOLR action).
- **Hindsight bias risk.** Without ex-ante stage calls documented, the framework becomes a retrospective ornament. The wiki's Quality Standards require this discipline: stage assignments must be made *contemporaneously*, written down at the time, with ex-ante calls vs ex-post outcomes tracked honestly.
- **Sub-cycles vs full cycles in crypto.** Crypto since 2013 has had four arguable cycles (2013, 2017, 2021, 2024–?). Are these *separate bubbles* (each with own displacement) or *one continuous bubble with bear-market rallies and frauds*? K's framework supports either reading. The wiki tracks this open question.
- **AI corpus limitation specifically for crypto.** Training corpora are 2020–2022 cycle dominated. Cross-cycle calibration via AI is unreliable. Apply the framework via human judgment with on-chain data, not via AI summarization.

### What's NOT in primary Ch 2 (= secondary research)

The 5-stage framework as commonly taught uses different phrasings than Ch 2's actual prose. Ch 2's sequence is:
- **Displacement** → **Boom (credit-fueled)** → **Euphoria (with overtrading sub-stage)** → **Distress** → **Revulsion/Panic**

Common textbook summaries add a separate "Credit Expansion" stage or use "Speculation" as Stage 2. The substance is identical; the wiki uses the canonical 5-label sequence for clarity, while flagging that it's a slight reorganization of Ch 2's actual prose.

## The Five-Stage Bubble Cycle (Summary)

The full framework, with crypto-carrier hypotheses per stage, is at [[foundation/market-wisdom/bubble-cycle-stages]]. Quick summary:

| Stage | One-line description | Crypto signature |
|-------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **1. Displacement** | Exogenous shock creates real new profit opportunity in at least one sector | New narrative + new chain TVL + new ETF flows + sector ETF inflow concentration |
| **2. Boom** | Credit expansion accommodates the displacement; asset prices rise; investment increases. Initially rational. | Stablecoin supply expanding; perp OI growing; funding moderately positive; lending borrow rates compressing |
| **3. Euphoria** | "This time is different"; outsiders pile in; speculation for capital gains; apparent leverage falls (concealing real fragility) | Funding >50bp/8h; basis >10% annualized; retail app rankings; new tokens raising hundreds of millions; NFT/memecoin parabolic |
| **4. Distress** | Insider/outsider flow imbalance peaks; first cracks (failure, fraud, sharp commodity move) | Funding flips negative; aggregate OI falling on weakness; first peripheral protocol failures; whale wallets net send-to-exchange |
| **5. Panic / Revulsion** | Door-shut panic; cascade selling; only terminates on price exhaustion, exchange halts, or LOLR action | Liquidation cascades on Coinglass; stablecoin de-pegs; CEX withdrawal queues; correlation → 1 across alts |

## Minsky's Three-Finance Taxonomy (Summary)

Full page: [[foundation/market-wisdom/minsky-three-finance]]. Quick summary:

| Regime | Operating income covers | Survives downturn? |
|--------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Hedge** | Both interest AND principal | Yes — most resilient |
| **Speculative** | Interest only; principal rolls | Only if rollover market open |
| **Ponzi** | Neither; both depend on new credit / asset sales | Only if asset prices keep rising |

The system's *composition* shifts pro-cyclically: longer boom → higher Ponzi share → larger population that can be tipped by a small adverse shock. This is Minsky's load-bearing claim about endogenous financial fragility.

## The Lender of Last Resort

A discrete framework Kindleberger evaluates extensively in Ch 2.

### Bagehot's Rule (1873)

In a financial panic, the central bank should:
1. **Lend freely** — to anyone with good collateral.
2. **At a high (penalty) rate** — to discourage abuse and ensure only those who genuinely need liquidity come.
3. **On good collateral** — to ensure solvency, not just liquidity, is being supported.

Modern scholarship (Conti-Brown, Sissoko) notes Bagehot's emphasis was disproportionately on *lend freely* — "good collateral" was much easier to satisfy in the 1870s mercantile context than is usually assumed.

### Kindleberger's argument

Kindleberger broadly favors LOLR action:

> "Whether a lender of last resort should provide liquidity to forestall a panic and the decline in prices of real estate and stocks has been debated extensively. Those who oppose the provision of liquidity from a lender of last resort argue that the knowledge that such credits will be available encourages speculation. Those who want a lender of last resort worry more about coping with the current crisis and reducing the likelihood that a liquidity crisis will cascade into a solvency crisis than they do about forestalling a future crisis." (Ch 2)

Kindleberger's resolution: **the moral-hazard concern is real but second-order**. The cost of a liquidity-crisis-cascading-into-solvency-crisis exceeds the cost of marginally encouraging future speculation. He's also pragmatic on **announcement effect**: confidence that money *can* be obtained reduces demand for liquidity — sometimes the announcement is the rescue.

### Domestic vs. international LOLR

> "In domestic crises, government or the central bank has responsibility to act as a lender of last resort. At the international level, there is neither a world government nor any world bank adequately equipped to serve as a lender of last resort. The International Monetary Fund has not met the expectations of its founders as a lender of last resort." (Ch 2)

This is the load-bearing observation for crypto.

### The crypto LOLR question (open)

There is **no LOLR by design** in the crypto system. That's the architecture's stated feature. The question for trading purposes is whether LOLR *de facto* exists.

| Candidate | LOLR-like actions | Capacity / credibility | Verdict |
|-----------|-------------------|------------------------|---------|
| **Tether (USDT issuer)** | Plumbing for USD-equivalent liquidity; weathered de-pegs without halting redemption | Reserves uncertain; transparency limited | Plumbing role, not policy-LOLR |
| **Major CEXes (Binance, Coinbase, OKX)** | Binance briefly considered FTX rescue Nov 2022; SAFU funds | Single-firm balance sheets; no aggregate backstop | Localized rescuer at best |
| **Treasury holders (MicroStrategy / Saylor)** | Anchor buyers in drawdowns; sentiment anchor | Single-balance-sheet exposure | Sentiment anchor, not LOLR |
| **The Fed (via macro liquidity)** | March 2020 zero rates + unlimited QE → indirectly bid up risk assets | Massive but indirect; no crypto mandate | Macro-scale LOLR via dollar liquidity, not crypto-specific |
| **Stablecoin issuers (Circle, Tether)** | Circle bailed by SVB FDIC resolution March 2023 | Bailout was for the bank, not Circle directly | Crypto benefited from TradFi LOLR, not its own |

**Wiki conclusion:** Crypto has no LOLR. Two implications:
1. Panic terminations in crypto must come from **price exhaustion** or **exchange halts**, not rescue announcements. (No surprise V-bottom from a Fed-pivot-like announcement.)
2. The cycle's **drawdown depths are likely larger** than equivalent TradFi cycles for the same displacement scale. The 2022 -77% BTC drawdown vs. 2008 -56% S&P drawdown is consistent with this.

For the trader's day/swing trading: the **panic-termination signal** in crypto is liquidation cascade exhaustion (visible on Coinglass) + funding deeply negative + on-chain panic-send to exchange peaking — not central-bank announcements. This is operationalized in [[trading/mechanisms/accumulation-distribution|the accumulation-distribution page's spring setup]].

## Notable Aphorisms (cite-worthy)

From Ch 2:
- "History is particular; economics is general."
- **"Details proliferate; structure abides."** (the validity defense)
- **"There is nothing as disturbing to one's well-being and judgment as to see a friend get rich. Unless it is to see a non-friend get rich."** (the FOMO mechanism)
- "The words differ across the countries but the tune is the same." (on "this time is different")
- "A bubble is an upward price movement over an extended period of fifteen to forty months that then implodes." (operational definition)
- "At every moment the purchases of real estate or stocks by the new investors or outsiders are necessarily balanced by sales by the insiders." (distress mechanic)
- **"The lenders had failed to ask the question 'Where will the borrowers get the cash to pay us the interest if we stop supplying them with the cash in the form of new loans?'"** (the Ponzi-finance diagnostic)
- "Investors seem not to have learned from experience." (closing line)
- "Revulsion and discredit may lead to panic (or as the Germans put it, *Torschlusspanik*, 'door-shut-panic') as investors crowd to get through the door before it slams shut."
- "The confidence that one can get money may be sufficient to reduce the demand for liquidity." (announcement-effect mechanic)

## Crypto Adaptation Notes

The framework transfers with high fidelity. Crypto exhibits a *more visible* version of the credit-cycle dynamics Kindleberger observes:

| Kindleberger phenomenon | Crypto carrier (today, observable) |
|--------------------|------------------------------------|
| Pro-cyclical credit supply | Stablecoin supply expansion/contraction (USDT, USDC); perp OI dynamics; lending-protocol borrow rates |
| Displacement (exogenous shock) | New ETF launches; protocol mechanism innovations; macro pivots; regulatory clarity events |
| Euphoria signatures | Funding rates persistently >50bp/8h; CT app-store rankings; influencer cycles; new-token issuance volume |
| Insider/outsider flow imbalance | On-chain whale flow vs. retail inflow patterns (Glassnode cohort analysis) |
| Distress trigger (swindle revelation) | Algorithmic stablecoin de-peg (Terra/UST May 2022); fund insolvency revelation (3AC June 2022); exchange fraud (FTX Nov 2022) |
| Panic termination | Liquidation cascade exhaustion + funding floor + on-chain panic-send peak — NOT central-bank rescue |
| LOLR architecture | None by design; the macro-liquidity Fed acts as indirect LOLR |
| Ponzi finance regime | On-chain identifiable: Anchor 19.5% APY vs UST stability mechanism; 3AC GBTC + stETH + Luna stack; CeFi yield platforms |

The deeper observation: where Kindleberger had to *infer* the credit-cycle dynamics from aggregated banking statistics with quarterly lag, crypto traders have **real-time visibility into the credit cycle** via stablecoin supply, perp OI, funding rates, and on-chain whale flow. The mechanism is identical (credit-supply pro-cyclicality drives bubble formation and bust); the **instrumentation has improved by orders of magnitude**.

This is one of the few places where modern crypto-native data strictly *improves* on the 1978 method. See [[foundation/market-structure/crypto-carrier-features]] for the central catalog.
