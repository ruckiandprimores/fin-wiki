# Commodity-Cyclical Valuation Inversion — Low P/E on Peak Earnings Is the Trap

> **TL;DR**: In a commodity cyclical, the normal value screen inverts — a LOW P/E on PEAK earnings
> is the danger signal, and the trough entry looks like a HIGH-or-negative P/E near book value.
> This page operationalizes [[trading/mechanisms/cyclical-timing|Lynch's P/E-inversion warning]]
> into a detection sequence: a P/B cross-check, a 4-part trough signature, and duration/narrative
> tells — plus the mirror-image case where the same logic runs backwards. *Analysis, not advice —
> this is a framework for reading a multiple against a cycle position, not a verdict on any name.*

## The Inversion (the Core Claim)

For a normal equity, the value screen is stable: low P/E is a candidate, high P/E is a warning. For
a commodity cyclical, the earnings line itself swings by an order of magnitude across the cycle, so
the same screen reads backwards at the extremes:

| | Normal equity | Commodity cyclical |
|---|---|---|
| Low P/E | Candidate — possible value | **Danger — the E is at peak; the market is refusing to capitalize it** |
| High / negative P/E | Avoid — expensive or unprofitable | **The entry — earnings are trough or negative** |
| The reliable anchor | Earnings power | **Book value / replacement cost** (earnings are meaningless at the extremes) |

**Why the multiple is doing its job, not failing at it:** a single-digit P/E in a boom is the
market *correctly* pricing the unsustainability of the E — not a mispricing waiting to be
arbitraged. This is [[trading/mechanisms/cyclical-timing|the same P/E-inversion mechanism Lynch
describes for equity cyclicals]] (Ford, Dow, steel), restated for commodity producers where the
underlying good (memory chips, oil, steel, lumber) trades on a visible spot/contract price that
makes the cycle position directly observable — no need to infer it from lagging financial
statements alone. Howard Marks' formulation of the same idea: **"deeply cyclical stocks look
cheapest when they are most expensive."** See [[foundation/sources/marks-mastering-the-cycle|Marks,
Mastering the Market Cycle]] for the full pendulum framing this borrows from.

## The P/B Cross-Check — the Fastest Diagnostic

**P/E and P/B disagree at cycle extremes, and P/B is the honest one.** When trailing or forward P/E
looks cheap while price-to-book is elevated relative to the name's own cyclical history, that
divergence — not either ratio alone — is the tell. A commodity producer's book value (or
replacement cost of its productive assets) is a far more stable anchor across the cycle than its
earnings, because earnings amplify small swings in the underlying commodity price through
operating leverage while the asset base does not.

**Rule of thumb for commodity producers**: names in deeply cyclical hardware/materials businesses
have historically bottomed **at or below ~1× book (replacement value)** — the point where the
market prices the business as worth no more than liquidating its physical assets. A high P/B
paired with a low P/E is the mirror-image signature of a healthy trough: it says the market is
capitalizing today's abnormal earnings power, not the durable asset base.

**Low P/E + high P/B on a commodity producer is the textbook peak signature.** This pairing is
the single fastest cross-check available before reaching for the fuller sequence below — cheap
on the number everyone quotes (P/E), expensive on the number that actually anchors the cycle
(P/B).

## The 4-Part Trough Sequence

All four tend to appear together, in roughly this order, at a genuine commodity-cyclical bottom:

1. **Contract/spot prices turn negative for 2+ consecutive quarters** — the cleanest cycle gauge
   available for a commodity business. The first negative print after a run of increases is the
   bell that starts the down-leg.
2. **Gross margins compress toward the teens/20s** (down from boom-time 50%+) — operating leverage
   working in reverse as fixed-cost-heavy production meets falling realized prices.
3. **Stock down ~40–60% from cycle highs, trading near ~1.5× book or below**, carrying a high or
   negative trailing P/E — the valuation signature described above, now confirmed by price action.
4. **Capex being CUT, not added** — the supply-side response that eventually ends every cycle.
   Producers finally curtailing new capacity is the leading indicator that the glut is being
   corrected; capex still expanding is a sign the down-leg has further to run.

**The trough will feel terrible and screen as expensive on trailing P/E. That is the signature, not
a disqualifier.** A screen that filters out anything with a high or negative trailing P/E will
systematically filter out every commodity-cyclical bottom.

## Duration and Narrative Tells

- **Duration**: if the current upcycle already matches or exceeds the length of the longest prior
  upcycle in the same commodity's history, that alone argues for a late-cycle read — cycle length
  is itself a base-rate input, independent of any single quarter's numbers.
- **"This time is different" appearing in print is itself a tell.** Historically, some version of
  this framing has preceded nearly every commodity-cyclical bust — the argument that a structural
  change (a new demand source, a technology shift, a capacity-discipline pact) has permanently
  altered the supply/demand balance. **Steelman it honestly before applying the discipline**: these
  arguments are sometimes partly right — structural shifts are real and *can* lengthen a cycle
  relative to its historical pattern. The correct response isn't to dismiss the narrative outright,
  it's to separate the defensible claim from the undefendable one: **"structurally tighter for
  longer" is a claim that can be true; "no longer cyclical" is the claim that has been the
  narrative at nearly every prior top.** The discipline is knowing which version of the argument is
  being made.
- **Insider behavior** — management publicly framing a structural regime change while insider
  transactions skew toward selling is a classic divergence between the story being told and the
  position being taken.
- **A credible new volume entrant** is historically how oligopoly-style pricing discipline in a
  commodity-hardware business breaks down, typically with roughly a multi-year lag between the
  entrant's emergence and its supply actually landing — a forward-looking supply signal distinct
  from anything visible in current financials.

## The Mirror Image — Do Not Over-Apply

**The same logic runs backwards in shallow, less-extreme cyclicals.** In a business with a milder
cycle amplitude, a HIGH P/E during a destocking downcycle can signal *opportunity* rather than
danger — trough earnings mechanically inflate the multiple, the same way peak earnings mechanically
deflate it in the commodity case above. The direction of the inversion is set by where the name
sits in its cycle, not by whether the multiple is numerically high or low in isolation.

**The rule is not "low P/E bad" or "high P/E good."** Both readings above — commodity peak and
shallow-cyclical trough — are cases of the identical underlying principle:

> **A multiple without a cycle position is not information.**

Applied without first establishing cycle position, this page's own logic produces exactly the
wrong conclusion in the mirror case. The P/B cross-check and the 4-part trough sequence exist
precisely because they establish cycle position independently of the P/E reading itself — that
independence is what makes them useful gates rather than restatements of the same number.

## Worked Examples (as of 2026-06/07 — perishable, illustration only)

These are examples of *applying the reasoning above*, not calls on any instrument — the same genre
as the `trading/rejected/` graveyard on the trading side: a record of how the diagnostic was
reasoned through, not a verdict to act on. Figures are dated snapshots and decay immediately;
treat them as illustration of method only.

- **Micron (June 2026)**: a review read its ~7–10× forward P/E against a ~10.5× price-to-book — a
  case of the P/B cross-check flagging the low-P/E-high-P/B divergence directly: the multiple
  looked cheap on what the review assessed as peak-cycle FY27 earnings expectations, while book
  value implied a very different read of where the name sat in its cycle.
- **SanDisk (July 2026)**: a more extreme version of the same pattern — ~9× forward P/E on what
  was read as peak earnings, against ~17× book, following a +635% year-to-date move. The
  P/E-vs-P/B divergence here was wider than in the first example, illustrating that the gap
  between the two ratios — not the P/E level alone — is what scales with how extreme the cycle
  position is.

The two examples are four months apart, same trap, independently encountered — the N=2 recurrence
that turned a session observation into this page.

Both examples are drawn from the memory/storage sub-industry, historically one of the most
extreme-amplitude commodity cyclicals in semiconductors — see the cyclicality-gradient discussion
in [[foundation/asset-classes/semiconductor-economics-primer|the semiconductor economics primer]]
for how memory compares to shallower-cycle analog/mixed-signal names, which is also where the
mirror-image case above becomes concretely relevant.

## Falsifier for the Framework Itself

If a commodity cyclical sustains peak-class margins through a full demand shock **without** a
supply-led price collapse — i.e., the "this time is different" structural claim proves out in
full, not just the defensible "tighter for longer" version — the framework needs revision for that
sub-class of commodity business. **As of 2026-07, that evidence does not exist for the memory
sub-industry**: no upcycle in memory's history has avoided an eventual supply-led correction, and
the current cycle has not yet been tested against a full demand shock. This falsifier is
sub-class-specific — a different commodity cyclical (e.g., one with genuinely different supply
elasticity or a durable oligopoly structure) would need its own falsifier check rather than
inheriting memory's history by default.

## Key Takeaways

- In a commodity cyclical, low P/E on peak earnings is the danger signal; high-or-negative P/E near
  book value is the entry signature — the inversion of the normal value screen.
- The P/E-vs-P/B divergence is the fastest cross-check: low P/E + high P/B on a commodity producer
  is the textbook peak.
- The 4-part trough sequence (negative contract prices 2+ quarters, margin compression to teens/20s,
  −40–60% price action near ~1.5× book, capex cuts) tends to arrive together, in that rough order.
- Cycle duration and "this time is different" narratives are tells in their own right — steelman
  the structural argument, then separate "tighter for longer" (defensible) from "no longer
  cyclical" (the top narrative).
- The logic mirrors in shallow cyclicals — a high P/E in a mild destocking downcycle can mean
  opportunity, not danger. The rule is never "low P/E bad"; it's "a multiple without a cycle
  position is not information."

## Open Questions

- **What is the equivalent of "capacity utilization" for less-visible commodity cyclicals** — i.e.,
  businesses where spot/contract pricing isn't as cleanly public as it is in memory chips? The
  4-part sequence leans on directly observable contract prices; adapting it to opaque-pricing
  cyclicals (some industrial materials, some agricultural inputs) needs a substitute leading
  indicator.
- **How reliably does the ~1× book trough floor hold across different commodity sub-industries?**
  The rule of thumb above is drawn primarily from memory/storage history — whether it generalizes
  to steel, lumber, or other commodity hardware businesses with different asset intensity and
  depreciation schedules is untested here.
- **Does the "new volume entrant breaks oligopoly discipline" lag length vary systematically with
  the entrant's home-market subsidy structure?** State-backed entrants (raised in the source task
  payload) may compress or extend the lag differently than purely commercial ones — open question
  for a future ingest.
