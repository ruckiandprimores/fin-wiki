# Inflation and Investments

> **TL;DR**: Stocks don't reliably hedge inflation in the short term. The historical record shows no consistent relationship between inflation and stock returns. Don't buy stocks *because* you fear inflation — buy them because they offer value.

## The Common Belief (And Its Problems)

The standard argument:
1. Inflation erodes the purchasing power of fixed-income investments
2. Therefore, bonds are "inherently undesirable"
3. Therefore, stocks are inherently better
4. Therefore, hold 100% stocks

**Graham's response:** This logic is too simple. It ignores price.

> "A statement of this kind would be as absurd as was the contrary one — too often heard years ago — that any bond is safer than any stock."

## The Historical Evidence

### US Inflation 1915-1970

| Period | Inflation | What Happened to Stocks |
|--------|-----------|------------------------|
| 1915-1920 | +96% (doubled!) | Stocks +20% — far below inflation |
| 1920-1925 | -16.5% (deflation) | Stocks +90% — did great |
| 1935-1940 | +0% | Stocks +28% |
| 1946-1950 | +30% | Stocks +40% — tracked inflation |
| 1965-1970 | +22% | Stocks DECLINED |

**The 1966-1970 paradox:** The rise in cost of living was 22% — the largest in a five-year period since WWII. Yet both stock earnings and stock prices *declined*.

> "There is no close time connection between inflationary (or deflationary) conditions and the movement of common-stock earnings and prices."

## Why Stocks Don't Automatically Hedge Inflation

### 1. Inflation Doesn't Raise Corporate Returns on Capital

> "The only way that inflation can add to common stock values is by raising the rate of earnings on capital investment. On the basis of the past record this has not been the case."

The data:
- 1950-1970: wholesale prices up 40%
- Corporate earnings ROE: did NOT increase; actually declined
- The entire growth in EPS came from reinvesting profits, not from inflation

**Translation:** Companies don't magically become more profitable during inflation. If anything, costs rise faster than pricing power for many businesses.

### 2. The Price You Pay Matters More

Even if stocks are better than bonds *in general*, that doesn't mean they're better *at any price*:

> "Our readers must have enough intelligence to recognize that even high-quality stocks cannot be a better purchase than bonds under all conditions — i.e., regardless of how high the stock market may be."

In the early 1970s:
- High-grade bonds yielded 7.5%+
- Dividend yields on stocks were ~3.5%
- Expected stock return: ~7.5% (dividends + appreciation)
- Bonds and stocks offered roughly equal expected returns

At that point, the "inflation protection" of stocks wasn't free — you weren't getting a premium for taking stock risk.

### 3. Short-Term Psychological Reality

> "On this point we can be categorical. There is no close time connection between inflationary conditions and the movement of common-stock earnings and prices."

Your investment horizon matters. Over 55 years, stocks beat bonds. Over 5 years? Either can win, regardless of inflation.

## The TIPS Alternative

Jason Zweig's update (2003):

> "Since 1997, when Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (or TIPS) were introduced, stocks have no longer been the automatically superior choice for investors who expect inflation to increase."

TIPS:
- Principal adjusts with CPI
- Guarantees real (inflation-adjusted) return
- No equity risk
- Removes the need to guess inflation vs. stock returns

**For investors who fear inflation:** TIPS are the cleaner hedge than hoping stocks will outperform.

## The Real Protection Against Inflation

Graham's actual recommendation:

> "We think it would be reasonable for an investor at this point to base his thinking and decisions on a probable rate of future inflation of, say, 3% per annum."

And then:
- Accept that bonds will lose some purchasing power
- Accept that stocks might not fully compensate
- Hold both — don't go 100% either way
- Focus on getting reasonable prices, not on macro forecasting

The real protection is **not overpaying**, not asset class selection.

## For Crypto (Adaptation)

The "crypto is an inflation hedge" narrative has similar problems:

1. **No consistent relationship** — Bitcoin has crashed during inflationary periods and risen during deflationary ones
2. **Price matters** — buying BTC at any price because "inflation" makes the same error Graham warns about
3. **The thesis is untested** — crypto hasn't existed through multiple full inflation cycles
4. **Gold comparison is weak** — gold has thousands of years of history; crypto has ~15 years

If you believe crypto hedges inflation, fine — but don't pay infinite prices for that belief.

## Key Quotes

> "On the basis of the past record we cannot count on inflation to automatically add to common stock values."

> "If inflation had operated as a separate favorable factor, its effect would have been to increase the 'value' of previously existing capital; this in turn should increase the rate of earnings on such old capital. But nothing of the kind actually happened in the past 20 years."

> "The investor cannot count on much above the recent five-year rate earned on the DJIA group — about 10% on net tangible assets."

> "The future of security prices is never predictable. Since you cannot predict the behavior of the markets, you must learn how to predict and control your own behavior."
