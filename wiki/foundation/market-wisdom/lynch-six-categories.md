# Lynch's Six Stock Categories

> **TL;DR**: Before deciding to buy, hold, or sell a stock, classify it. Lynch argues every public company falls into exactly one of six buckets — slow grower, stalwart, fast grower, cyclical, turnaround, asset play — and each demands a different playbook. Misclassification is one of the most expensive mistakes an investor makes.

## The Six Categories

| Category | Earnings growth | Typical example (Lynch's era) | What you're buying |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| **Slow grower** | ~GNP rate (3%) | Houston Industries (utility) | Steady dividend, low volatility |
| **Stalwart** | 10–12% | Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola, Bristol-Myers | Recession resilience, modest multibagger |
| **Fast grower** | 20–25%+ | Wal-Mart, Marriott, Limited (during expansion) | Tenbagger / 40-bagger potential |
| **Cyclical** | Volatile, trough-to-peak | Ford, Dow, AMR, USX | Earnings cycle timing |
| **Turnaround** | Negative → recovery | Chrysler 1982, Penn Central, Con Ed | Post-distress re-rating |
| **Asset play** | Hidden value | Pebble Beach, Newhall, Alico, Penn Central | Sum-of-parts > stock price |

## How Lynch Defines Each

### Slow Growers

> "Usually these large and aging companies are expected to grow slightly faster than the gross national product. Slow growers didn't start out that way. They started out as fast growers and eventually pooped out."

**Identifiers:**
- Chart looks like "the topographical map of Delaware" (Lynch) — flat with no hills
- Pays a "generous and regular dividend" — sign that management can't reinvest profitably
- Industry has matured (utilities, autos in mature markets, mainframe computers post-1980s)

**Lynch's verdict:** Mostly avoid. "What's the sense of wasting time on sluggards?"

### Stalwarts

> "Multibillion-dollar hulks are not exactly agile climbers, but they're faster than slow growers. When you traffic in stalwarts, you're more or less in the foothills: 10 to 12 percent annual growth in earnings."

**Identifiers:**
- Large established consumer franchises (Coca-Cola, Hershey's, Bristol-Myers)
- Recession-proof products — Bristol-Myers had **only one down quarter in twenty years**; Kellogg none in thirty
- Steady but unspectacular price action

**Lynch's verdict:** "Stocks that I generally buy for a 30 to 50 percent gain, then sell and repeat the process with similar issues that haven't yet appreciated." Recession ballast — "good friends in a crisis."

### Fast Growers

> "These are among my favorite investments: small, aggressive new enterprises that grow at 20 to 25 percent a year. If you choose wisely, this is the land of the 10- to 40-baggers, and even the 200-baggers."

**Identifiers:**
- Grow by replicating a winning formula — Wal-Mart store-by-store, Marriott city-by-city, The Limited mall-by-mall, Taco Bell location-by-location
- **Doesn't need to be in a fast-growing industry.** Anheuser-Busch was a fast grower in the slow-growing beer industry by taking market share.
- Strong balance sheet, substantial profits

**Lynch's verdict:** The trick is "figuring out when they'll stop growing, and how much to pay for the growth." Sell when same-store sales decline or geographic saturation hits.

### Cyclicals

> "A cyclical is a company whose sales and profits rise and fall in regular if not completely predictable fashion. Charts of the cyclicals look like the polygraphs of liars, or the maps of the Alps, as opposed to the maps of Delaware you get with the slow growers."

**Identifiers:**
- Industry tied to business cycle: autos, airlines, tires, steel, chemicals, defense
- Wide P/E swings — appears cheap at the peak (high E), expensive at the trough (low E)
- Often confused with stalwarts because they're large, blue-chip, well-known

**Lynch's verdict:** "Timing is everything." Most-misunderstood category. Misclassifying Ford as a stalwart and buying at the cycle peak can lose 80%. *(See [[trading/mechanisms/cyclical-timing]] for the timing playbook.)*

### Turnarounds

> "These aren't slow growers; these are no growers. These aren't cyclicals that rebound; these are potential fatalities, such as Chrysler."

**Five sub-types Lynch identifies:**
1. **Bail-us-out-or-else** — Chrysler 1982, Lockheed (government loan guarantees)
2. **Who-would-have-thunk-it** — Con Ed (utility going from $10 to $3 to $52)
3. **Little problem we didn't anticipate** — Three Mile Island / GPU
4. **Perfectly good company inside a bankrupt company** — Toys "R" Us (out of Interstate Department Stores; 57-bagger)
5. **Restructuring to maximize shareholder values** — Penn Central (the "ultimate asset play turnaround")

**Lynch's verdict:** "The best thing about investing in successful turnarounds is that of all the categories of stocks, their ups and downs are least related to the general market." Avoid the open-ended-liability turnarounds (Bhopal/Union Carbide, Johns-Manville).

### Asset Plays

> "An asset play is any company that's sitting on something valuable that you know about, but that the Wall Street crowd has overlooked."

**Asset types Lynch found:**
- Real estate (Pebble Beach — gravel pit alone worth more than market cap; Newhall Land; Alico)
- Cash piles
- Tax-loss carryforwards (Penn Central post-bankruptcy)
- Subscriber bases / cable franchises (Telecommunications, Inc. — 250-bagger)
- Brand value not on the balance sheet
- Spinoff potential (TV stations inside an insurance company — Liberty Corp.)

**Lynch's verdict:** "The asset play is where the local edge can be used to greatest advantage." Sell when the raider arrives.

## The Critical Insight: Categories Move

A stock's category is not permanent. Lynch's recurring warning:

- **Fast growers become slow growers.** Alcoa, plastic-era Dow, electric utilities of the 1960s — all once go-go growth, all slowed. "Numerous analysts and prognosticators are fooled. There's always a tendency to think that things will never change."
- **Stalwarts can become turnarounds** if they go too far (Avon stalwart-at-50×-earnings → 80% drawdown).
- **Turnarounds, after they turn around, must be re-categorized.** Chrysler at $48 in 1987 was no longer a turnaround — it was a cyclical. "If the turnaround has been successful, you have to reclassify the stock."
- **Cyclicals can become turnarounds** when poorly managed (Chrysler 1982 = cyclical-turned-turnaround).

**Operational implication:** every 6–12 months, re-check your holdings against the category criteria. If category has shifted, the buy/hold/sell rule must shift with it.

## Why Categorization Is the Whole Game

Each category implies a *completely different* analytical focus:

| Category | What you analyze | What kills you |
|----------|------------------|---------------|
| Slow grower | Dividend safety, payout ratio | Dividend cut |
| Stalwart | P/E vs historical band, recession durability | Buying at top of P/E band (Avon trap) |
| Fast grower | Same-store sales, expansion runway | End of phase 2 expansion |
| Cyclical | Where in cycle? Inventory, commodity prices, capacity utilization | Mistiming entry |
| Turnaround | Cash to survive, debt paydown, share dilution risk | Dilution wipes out per-share recovery (Navistar) |
| Asset play | Sum-of-parts vs market cap, raider activity | Asset sold below estimated value, dilutive funding |

A single P/E rule (say, "buy below 15") is wrong for at least three categories.

## Crypto Translation (Hypothesis — Needs Validation)

This is the speculative cross-market translation. The category structure transfers; the specific identifiers need adaptation.

| Lynch category | Crypto candidate | What replaces "earnings growth"? |
|----------------|------------------|------------------------------------|
| Slow grower | BTC, ETH (mature L1s) | Network value vs realized cap stable; supply emission slowing |
| Stalwart | Top DEXes, lending protocols (UNI, AAVE) | Steady fee revenue; recession-resilient utility |
| Fast grower | New L1/L2 launches, new vertical-specific protocols | Verifiable on-chain usage growth (TVL ex-self-deposits, fees, addresses) |
| Cyclical | Almost everything — BTC dominance cycle drives alt rotation | Position in BTC cycle (halving phase); funding regime |
| Turnaround | Post-exploit recovery (Mango, Curve), post-bear-cycle revivals | Path to re-attaining pre-incident usage; legal/exploit cleanup status |
| Asset play | DAOs with treasury > market cap; protocols with strategic IP | Treasury-to-market-cap ratio; verifiable value-on-chain |

**Open questions for the wiki to explore:**
- Does the four-year halving cycle make *all* of crypto a cyclical?
- What is crypto's analog of "same-store sales decline" — the signal that a fast grower is decelerating?
- Are stablecoin issuers stalwarts or asset plays?

## Key Takeaways

- Always classify before deciding action — different categories demand different rules
- Categories are not permanent; revisit classification regularly
- The most-dangerous mistake: treating a cyclical like a stalwart
- The biggest wins come from fast growers and turnarounds; the steadiest from stalwarts; the least from slow growers
- Lynch's per-category sell rules are crucial — see [[investment/allocation/when-to-sell-by-category]]

## Tension with Graham (flagged 2026-04-29)

Lynch's enthusiasm for "fast growers" — and his claim that they're "the land of 10- to 40-baggers" — sits in apparent tension with [[investment/allocation/enterprising-investor]], where Graham warns that "obvious prospects for physical growth do not translate into obvious profits for investors" (the airline-stocks lesson).

**Resolution:** the contradiction is more apparent than real. Graham's anti-growth stance is specifically about **paying for growth that's already priced in.** Lynch agrees — he refuses fast growers when their P/E significantly exceeds growth rate (his PEG-style discipline: "a 20-percent grower selling at 20 times earnings is a much better buy than a 10-percent grower selling at 10 times earnings, but only because their PEs are equal to their growth rates"). Both authors converge on: *don't pay more for growth than the growth itself justifies.* They differ on **how investable quality growth at premium-but-reasonable prices is** — Graham says rarely, Lynch says often if you do the work.

**Deeper resolution (added 2026-04-29 after Buffett ingest):** Buffett's framing in [[foundation/market-wisdom/value-vs-growth-false-dichotomy]] dissolves the dichotomy entirely. Growth is not a separate strategy from value investing — growth is a *variable in the value calculation* whose contribution can be positive, neutral, or negative depending on whether returns on retained capital exceed cost of capital. Both Graham (anti-low-quality-growth) and Lynch (pro-high-quality-growth) reduce to the same question: *is this growth value-creating?* The framing is no longer "growth vs. value" but "value calculation including growth as a variable."
