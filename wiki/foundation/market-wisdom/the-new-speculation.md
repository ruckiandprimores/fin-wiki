# The New Speculation in Common Stocks

> **TL;DR**: Graham's 1958 speech identifying a structural shift: speculation used to reside in *companies* (weak balance sheets, uncertain industries). Now it resides in *valuations* — the prices investors pay for even the best companies create the speculative risk. The more you focus on growth expectations, the more uncertain your outcomes.

## The Thesis

From Graham's 1958 lecture to the National Federation of Financial Analysts:

> "In the past the speculative elements of a common stock resided almost exclusively in the company itself — they were due to uncertainties, or fluctuating elements, or downright weaknesses in the industry, or the corporation's individual setup. These elements of speculation still exist, of course; but it may be said that they have been sensibly diminished by a number of long-term developments. But in revenge a new and major element of speculation has been introduced into the common-stock arena from outside the companies. It comes from the attitude and viewpoint of the stock-buying public and their advisers."

**The key distinction:**
- **Old speculation (endogenous):** Resides in the company — weak finances, volatile industry, uncertain earnings
- **New speculation (exogenous):** Resides in the valuation — prices so high that even great companies become risky

## The Pennsylvania Railroad vs. American Can (1911-1913)

Graham's illustrative example:

### Pennsylvania Railroad (Investment)
- Price: 53-65 (narrow range)
- P/E: 12-15 times
- Dividend: $3 (steady)
- Book value: $50+ (tangible, real)

### American Can (Speculation)
- Price: 9-47 (volatile)
- Earnings: 7 cents to $8.86
- P/E: 1.9 to 10 times
- Dividend: none
- Book value: "water" — par value had no tangible backing

**In 1913:** Investors knew American Can was speculative because the *company* was speculative — uncertain industry, no dividend record, no asset backing.

**Irony:** American Can had a far better long-term future than Pennsylvania Railroad. But in 1913, that future was "basically irrelevant to investment policies."

## The IBM Story (Graham's Personal)

Graham's confession:

> "In 1916 I went to the head of my firm... and pointed out that C.-T.-R. stock [later IBM] was selling at 40s... had earned $6.50... had book value of $130... started a $3 dividend... I thought highly of the company's products and prospects."

His boss's response:

> "'Ben,' said he, 'do not mention that company to me again. I would not touch it with a ten-foot pole. Its 6 percent bonds are selling in the low 80s and they are no good. So how can the stock be any good? Everybody knows there is nothing behind it but water.'"

Graham never bought IBM in his life.

**The lesson:** The old-time investor paid *only* for the present — tangible assets, current dividends, proven earnings. The future was free.

## The Reversal of Value

### Old Approach (Pre-1920s)
- Tangible assets valued at 6-8% required return
- Intangibles (goodwill, growth) valued at 15%+ required return
- You got a *discount* on future prospects
- Speculators paid for the future; investors got it free

### New Approach (Post-1950s)
- Tangible assets valued at 10% required return (need to earn 10% ROE to sell at book)
- Intangibles valued at *lower* required return (higher multiples)
- You pay a *premium* for future prospects
- The more profitable a company, the higher the multiple — and the higher the speculation

> "Companies that earn a high return on capital are given these liberal appraisals not only because of the good profitability itself... but perhaps even more cogently because high earnings on capital generally go hand in hand with a good growth record and prospects."

## The Mathematics Problem

> "Given the three ingredients of (a) optimistic assumptions as to the rate of earnings growth, (b) a sufficiently long projection of this growth into the future, and (c) the miraculous workings of compound interest — lo! the security analyst is supplied with a new kind of philosopher's stone which can produce or justify any desired valuation for a really 'good stock.'"

**Graham's warning about math:**

> "In forty-four years of Wall Street experience and study I have never seen dependable calculations made about common-stock values, or related investment policies, that went beyond simple arithmetic or the most elementary algebra. Whenever calculus is brought in, or higher algebra, you could take it as a warning signal that the operator was trying to substitute theory for experience, and usually also to give to speculation the deceptive guise of investment."

## The U-Shaped Risk Curve

Graham's visual model:

```
Speculative Risk
     |
High |  *                                    *
     |   \                                  /
     |    \                                /
     |     \                              /
Low  |      \____________*_______________/
     |
     +----------------------------------------
          Low           Medium          High
          Credit        Credit          Credit
          Rating        Rating          Rating
```

- **Left (low credit):** High risk because company is weak
- **Middle (solid companies):** Lowest risk — well-established, reasonable prices
- **Right (best companies):** High risk returns — prices so high they create speculation

> "At the right extremity, where the company has the highest credit rating because both its past record and future prospects are most impressive, we find that the stock market tends more or less continuously to introduce a highly speculative element into the common shares through the simple means of a price so high as to carry a fair degree of risk."

## The Shakespeare Quotation

Graham found this "surprisingly relevant":

> *"Have I not seen dwellers on form and favor*
> *Lose all and more by paying too much rent?"*

**Translation:** People who focus on quality ("form and favor") can still lose by paying too much.

## Medius Tutissimus Ibis

Graham concludes with Ovid's advice:

> *"Medius tutissimus ibis"*
> "You will go safest in the middle course."

**Practical implication:** The middle-ground stocks — solid companies, not glamorous, reasonably priced — offer the lowest speculative risk. They're overlooked because everyone focuses on growth stocks or distressed situations.

## For Crypto (Adaptation)

The "new speculation" is amplified in crypto:

1. **No "old speculation" baseline** — most crypto projects have *no* tangible assets, *no* earnings, *no* dividend history. They start as 100% speculative on the company side.

2. **Price creates all the speculation** — buying ETH at $100 vs $4000 involves the same underlying protocol but wildly different speculative risk.

3. **Math and projections everywhere** — Token valuation models (Stock-to-Flow, Metcalfe's Law) are exactly the "higher mathematics" Graham warned about.

4. **The best projects have the highest prices** — BTC, ETH, SOL trade at the highest valuations precisely because they're "the best." This is the U-shaped curve at work.

**The middle course in crypto:** Second-tier protocols with real usage, reasonable valuations, less narrative hype. (Hard to identify, but that's where the lowest speculative risk should be.)

## Key Quotes

> "Today, if the investor is shrewd or well advised, he too must have his lookout on the future... Thus it was the speculator who looked out and saw future developments coming before other people did. But today... he mounts into a common lookout where he rubs elbows with the speculator."

> "The combination of precise formulas with highly imprecise assumptions can be used to establish, or rather to justify, practically any value one wishes, however high, for a really outstanding issue."

> "It follows as a kind of mathematical law that the more important the good will or future earning-power factor the more uncertain becomes the true value of the enterprise, and therefore the more speculative inherently the common stock."

> "Today's investor is so concerned with anticipating the future that he is already paying handsomely for it in advance. Thus what he has projected with so much study and care may actually happen and still not bring him any profit."
