# Dow Theory and the Three Premises of Technical Analysis

> **TL;DR**: The philosophical foundation of every chart-based trading system in use today. Charles Dow's editorials (1900-1902) → S.A. Nelson's compilation (1903) → Hamilton's *Stock Market Barometer* (1922) → Robert Rhea's *Dow Theory* (1932) → Murphy's modern formalization (1999). The three TA premises (market discounts everything; prices move in trends; history repeats) and the six Dow tenets are the structural assumptions that every technical trader operates under, whether they know it or not.

## The Three Premises of Technical Analysis

Per Murphy (1999, Ch 1):

> "There are three premises on which the technical approach is based:
> 1. Market action discounts everything.
> 2. Prices move in trends.
> 3. History repeats itself."

If you reject any of these, technical analysis is incoherent. If you accept them, the entire toolkit follows.

### 1. Market action discounts everything

> "The technician believes that anything that can possibly affect the price — fundamentally, politically, psychologically, or otherwise — is actually reflected in the price of that market. It follows, therefore, that a study of price action is all that is required."

This is the *strongest* claim in TA. It says price is a sufficient summary of all knowable information. Murphy explicitly notes the parallel to the academic Efficient Market Hypothesis: "Without meaning to, the academics have very eloquently stated the need for closely monitoring price action."

**Tension flag:** Buffett's [[foundation/market-wisdom/modern-finance-theory-critique|critique of EMH]] disagrees — markets are *frequently* inefficient. Both views can coexist if framed correctly: Murphy's claim is operational ("price summarizes most of what matters for short-term trading"); Buffett's claim is aspirational ("price misses long-term fundamentals you can exploit"). Different time horizons → different operational stances.

### 2. Prices move in trends

> "The whole purpose of charting the price action of a market is to identify trends in early stages of their development for the purpose of trading in the direction of those trends."

The Newton's-first-law corollary:

> "A trend in motion is more likely to continue than to reverse."

This is the **load-bearing claim** for trend-following strategies. Empirically, the momentum anomaly (12-month price momentum predicting future returns) is one of the most-replicated effects in academic finance — though the magnitude shrinks after costs and the persistence varies by regime.

**For crypto:** trend persistence is among the strongest of any asset class observed (BTC's 4-year halving cycles; alt rotations; stablecoin supply trends). Premise #2 is empirically *more* solid in crypto than in mature equities.

### 3. History repeats itself

> "Chart patterns reveal the bullish or bearish psychology of the market. Since these patterns have worked well in the past, it is assumed that they will continue to work well in the future. They are based on the study of human psychology, which tends not to change."

The pattern recognition foundation. The same crowd dynamics (FOMO, capitulation, accumulation) produce the same shapes on charts in 1929, 1987, 2008, 2022. A head & shoulders is a head & shoulders.

**Empirical caveat:** Bulkowski's *Encyclopedia of Chart Patterns* gives empirical pattern-completion rates. They are above random — but not by huge margins. The pattern is *suggestive*, not deterministic.

## Dow Theory — The Six Tenets

Per Murphy Ch 2 (drawing on Hamilton 1922, Rhea 1932):

### Tenet 1: The Averages Discount Everything

The Dow Industrial Average and the Dow Transportation Average reflect everything knowable. The market acts on news before it's released because somebody always knows.

> "The markets quickly discount such occurrences, and almost instantaneously assimilate their affects into the price action."

### Tenet 2: The Market Has Three Trends

- **Major (primary)** — lasts >1 year, the "tide"
- **Intermediate (secondary)** — lasts 3 weeks to 3 months, the "wave"
- **Minor (near-term)** — lasts <3 weeks, the "ripple"

Each lower-order trend exists *within* the higher order. A bull market has corrections; corrections have rallies; rallies have intraday selloffs.

**Crypto note**: the same fractal structure applies, with shorter periods. BTC bull markets (~18 months), corrections (1-3 months), pullbacks (1-3 weeks). The fractal layers compress because crypto runs 24/7 and faster.

### Tenet 3: Major Trends Have Three Phases

1. **Accumulation phase** — informed buyers acquire from despondent sellers. Press is uniformly bearish. Volume low.
2. **Public participation phase** — trend becomes visible; technicians enter; financial press turns positive. Most of the trend's price action happens here.
3. **Distribution phase** — informed sellers distribute to euphoric public buyers. Press is uniformly bullish; financial advisors universally optimistic. Volume often peaks here.

> "Students of Elliott Wave Theory will recognize this division of a major bull market into three distinct phases."

This maps directly to Wyckoff's accumulation/distribution framework. *(Wyckoff is a top-priority unfilled source per [[foundation/sources/lineage]].)*

**Crypto application — the most actionable tenet:**
- Accumulation: post-bear-market periods, sentiment at "crypto is dead" levels, BTC dominance rising
- Public participation: alt season, mainstream press coverage, ETF inflows accelerating
- Distribution: sentiment at extreme greed, retail FOMO peak, "your taxi driver is buying SHIB"

### Tenet 4: The Averages Must Confirm Each Other

Dow's original claim: bull/bear signals require both the Industrial Average *and* the Transportation Average to break out in the same direction. If one signals while the other doesn't, the prior trend is still valid.

The economic intuition: industrial production must move goods (transports) for an upturn to be real.

**Crypto translation:** the modern equivalents are:
- BTC ↔ ETH must confirm — if BTC is making new highs but ETH is failing, the rally is suspect
- Spot ↔ futures must confirm — strong spot accumulation with negative funding signals stronger underlying demand than spot up + positive funding (which is leverage-driven)
- Spot ↔ on-chain must confirm — exchange outflows + long-term holder accumulation confirm a price uptrend; their absence makes a price uptrend fragile

This is the most directly tradable Dow tenet for crypto.

### Tenet 5: Volume Must Confirm the Trend

> "Volume should expand or increase in the direction of the major trend... In a major uptrend, volume would then increase as prices move higher, and diminish as prices fall. In a downtrend, volume should increase as prices drop and diminish as they rally."

The mechanism: real demand (or supply) shows up as volume. A price move on declining volume is suspect because it's not backed by participation.

This is the foundation of all volume-based confirmation work. *(Expanded in [[trading/mechanisms/volume-analysis]].)*

### Tenet 6: A Trend Is Assumed to Be in Effect Until It Gives Definite Signals That It Has Reversed

The Newton's-first-law tenet at the major-trend level. A bull market is a bull market until the structure (rising peaks and rising troughs) is broken.

The corollary:

> "An object in motion (in this case a trend) tends to continue in motion until some external force causes it to change direction."

Practical implication: don't try to predict tops and bottoms. Wait for the structure to break — a lower high in an uptrend, a higher low in a downtrend.

## Limitations Murphy Acknowledges

> "Some Criticisms of Dow Theory" (Ch 2 section)

1. **Late signals** — Dow's signals typically come after 20-25% of the move has already happened. The major-trend reversal isn't confirmed until a meaningful move has already occurred.
2. **Subjectivity in identifying secondary corrections** — when does a pullback become a new trend?
3. **The Industrial / Transportation pairing is dated** — the modern economy has more sectors that need to confirm; sector-rotation analysis subsumes Dow's pairing.
4. **Doesn't address crypto-specific fragmentation** (my add) — Dow's framework assumes one or two indices summarize "the market." Crypto has BTC, ETH, alts as multiple regime-defining indices simultaneously.

## Key Takeaways

- The three TA premises (discounts everything; trends; history repeats) are necessary assumptions for any chart-based system
- Dow's six tenets are the foundational tradable claims — they get refined into specific mechanisms in the trading branch
- Tenet 3 (accumulation/public-participation/distribution) and Tenet 4 (averages must confirm) are the most directly tradable for crypto
- Murphy's framework is *complementary* to the value-investing trilogy, not in opposition — they describe different time horizons and different aspects of market behavior
