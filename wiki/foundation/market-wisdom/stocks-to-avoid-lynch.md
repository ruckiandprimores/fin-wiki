# Lynch's Stocks to Avoid

> **TL;DR**: The mirror image of the [[foundation/market-wisdom/perfect-stock-attributes|13 attributes]]. Lynch's avoid list is *behaviorally-driven losers* — stocks marketed at the most retail-friendly emotion (the next big thing, the exciting name, the whisper from a friend) and structurally rigged to underperform. Each pattern has a clear mechanism for why it loses.

## The Five Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | What it is | Why it loses |
|--------------|-----------|--------------|
| **The Next Something** | "The next IBM/McDonald's/Disney/Tesla" | Original company curses both — the next-of fails, *and* the original loses prestige |
| **Diworseification** | Profitable company spending cash on unrelated acquisitions | Management's circle of competence fails outside core; overpays |
| **Whisper Stock** | Hot tip with great story but no earnings | Stories without substance; companies that don't yet exist as businesses |
| **Middleman** | 25–50%+ of revenue from a single customer | Customer cancellation or price cut is catastrophic |
| **Exciting Name** | Trendy ticker / acronym / "advanced/leading/micro" branding | Marketed to retail; flashy name in mediocre company |

## (1) Beware the Next Something

> "When people tout a stock as the next of something, it often marks the end of prosperity not only for the imitator but also for the original to which it is being compared."

The mechanism is twofold:
- **For the imitator:** by definition, derivative — they're chasing where the original got rich, not finding their own niche. Late entrants in a winning category face saturated markets and brand dilution.
- **For the original:** when "the next IBM" gets called, the original IBM tends to be peaking. The narrative attention itself signals competitive entry, which compresses margins.

Lynch's body count:
- After Circuit City (the successful electronics retailer) — the "nexts" were First Family, Good Guys, Highland Superstores, Crazy Eddie, Fretters. **All lost between 59% and 96% of value.**
- After Toys "R" Us — Child World stumbled
- After Price Club — Warehouse Club fared no better

> "How many times have you heard that some player is supposed to be the next Willie Mays, only to find that he's cut from the team?"

**Crypto translation:** "The Solana killer," "The Ethereum killer," "The Coinbase of [country]," "The Robinhood of [niche]." Almost universally underperforming. The narrative is itself the warning sign.

## (2) Avoid Diworseifications

Lynch's coined term. *(See [[glossary/diworseification]].)*

> "The dedicated diworseifier seeks out merchandise that is (1) overpriced, and (2) completely beyond his or her realm of understanding. This ensures that losses will be maximized."

The pattern: a profitable company has cash. Instead of buying back shares or raising dividends, it:
1. Acquires a company in an unrelated industry
2. Pays a premium to get the deal done
3. Mismanages the acquisition because management lacks domain knowledge
4. Eventually writes it off — sometimes "explaining how it got out of a losing business before anybody realized it had gotten into the business in the first place" (Gillette + digital watches)
5. Then "restructures" — selling off the diworseification at less than purchase price

**Lynch's body count:**
- Mobil's purchase of Marcor (which owned Montgomery Ward); Mobil up only 10% from 1980 oil peak vs. Exxon up 100% (Exxon resisted diworseification, bought back stock)
- Gillette: digital watches, cosmetics, toiletries, pens, lighters, curlers, blenders — stock at $35 instead of $100+ if it had stuck to razors
- General Mills owned Chinese restaurants, Italian restaurants, steak houses, toys, shirts, coins, stamps, travel companies
- US Industries: 300 acquisitions in a single year
- Genesco: 150 acquisitions 1956–1973 → bankruptcy fundamentals; Melville (single related shoe→retail evolution) → 30-bagger

**The synergy fallacy:** "Synergy" is "a fancy name for the two-plus-two-equals-five theory." Sometimes works (Marriott + Big Boy restaurants → genuine operational synergy). Usually doesn't (Marriott + auto parts would not).

**Operational rule:** when a profitable company you own announces an acquisition outside its core, increase your scrutiny — and consider it a sell signal unless the synergy is mechanically obvious.

**Crypto translation:** Protocol token with $X in revenue announces a "rebrand" or "ecosystem expansion" — usually a token-pumping marketing exercise to mask deteriorating fundamentals. Layer-1 chains "launching consumer apps." Lending protocols building "social" features. Same pattern, faster cycle.

## (3) Beware the Whisper Stock

> "The longshots, also known as whisper stocks, and the whiz-bang stories. They probably reach your neighborhood about the same time they reach mine: the company that sells papaya juice derivative as a cure for slipped-disc pain."

The pattern: friend-of-friend tells you about an obscure company "on the brink of solving" the latest national problem (oil shortage, drug addiction, AIDS, climate change, AI safety). The story is either "very imaginative" or "impressively complicated."

The structural problem:
- Usually no earnings → no P/E → no fundamental anchor for valuation
- "Microscopes, Ph.D.'s, high hopes, and cash from the stock sale"
- Story can be elaborate enough that disconfirming evidence takes years to accumulate

Lynch's body count (small sample):
- Worlds of Wonder, Pizza Time Theater, One Potato Two (SPUD), National Health Care ($14 → $0.50), Sun World Airways ($8 → $0.50), Alhambra Mines (no actual mine), MGF Oil, American Surgery Centers, Asbetec Industries
- KMS Industries: 1980 "amorphous silicon photovoltaics" → 1984 "video multiplexer" → 1985 "spherical implosions" → 1986 "inertial confinement fusion." Stock $40 → $2.50.
- Bioresponse: cow lymph for diagnostic tests. Bought $9.25, sold at small loss after seeing the operation.

> "If the prospects are so phenomenal, then this will be a fine investment next year and the year after that. Why not put off buying the stock until later, when the company has established a record? Wait for the earnings."

**The IPO twist:** Lynch is wary of brand-new-enterprise IPOs. Three out of four are long-term disappointments. He has more luck with *spinoff* IPOs — companies with an existing track record being spun out (Toys "R" Us, Agency Rent-A-Car, Safety-Kleen).

**Crypto translation:** Whisper stocks are *most* of crypto launches. The pre-launch "story-only" token is the whisper stock par excellence. Substitute "no earnings" with "no usage / no real users." Lynch's rule applies: if the story is real, you can buy it next year too — but most stories aren't.

## (4) Beware the Middleman

> "The company that sells 25 to 50 percent of its wares to a single customer is in a precarious situation."

The mechanism:
- **Cancellation risk:** the big customer can decide to make the parts in-house (IBM ↔ SCI Systems risk)
- **Price-cut leverage:** even short of cancellation, the big customer can extract concessions that compress supplier margins to nothing
- **Concentration is structural fragility** — single point of failure

Lynch's example: disk-drive companies like Tandon, "always on the brink of disaster because they were too dependent on a few clients."

**Crypto translation:**
- Validator businesses dependent on a single staking protocol's fee model
- Bridge protocols where one chain integration is most of the volume
- DAO service providers (security firms, market-makers) where a single client is >50% of revenue
- Mining pools dependent on a single chain that may fork or transition (ETH PoW → PoS extinction event for ETH miners)

## (5) Beware the Stock with the Exciting Name

> "It's too bad that Xerox didn't have a name like David's Dry Copies, because then more people would have been skeptical of it."

The mechanism: marketing-driven naming generates retail demand without fundamentals. The flashy name signals management is more focused on perception than on business. Patterns:
- Acronyms: "UAL changed its name to Allegis hoping to appeal to modern trendy thinkers"
- "Advanced," "leading," "micro," "techno," "holdings," anything-with-an-X
- Mystifying names that "sound smart"

**Counter-example:** Crown, Cork, & Seal kept their boring industrial name despite consultant pressure to become "CroCorSea" — and rewarded shareholders for decades.

**Crypto translation:** Tokens with "Inu," "Coin," "Finance," "Protocol," "DeFi," "Chain," "AI" are often *more* suspicious than tokens with technical or boring names. The naming signals the marketing strategy. Compare: $UNI (boring abbreviation) vs. $TURBO ([narrative-driven name]).

## Two Additional Lynch Cautions Often Cited

### "When in Doubt, Tune in Later"

> "If the prospects are so phenomenal, then this will be a fine investment next year and the year after that. Why not put off buying the stock until later, when the company has established a record? Wait for the earnings."

The patience tax — buying the same company a year later, after it has either delivered or imploded — is small relative to the survival benefit of skipping the implosion cases.

### Pressure to Buy at the IPO

> "Often with the exciting longshots the pressure builds to buy at the initial public offering (IPO) or else you're too late."

Almost always false urgency. Lynch's data: 3 out of 4 brand-new-enterprise IPOs disappoint long-term. Apple was an exception; Genentech (1980) was an exception; most were not.

**Crypto translation:** IDO/IEO/launch sale FOMO. The "if you don't buy at TGE you've missed it" framing. Empirically false for most launches — most are below TGE pricing within months.

## Key Takeaways

- Each anti-pattern has a *mechanism* for why it loses, not just empirical loss history
- "The next something" loses both the imitator and the original
- "Diworseification" — increase scrutiny when a profitable company spends cash outside its core
- "Whisper stocks" — story without earnings; wait for the earnings, you can buy later
- "Middleman" — customer concentration >25% is structural fragility
- "Exciting names" — marketing-driven naming correlates with mediocre fundamentals
- The unifying meta-rule: **anti-patterns are emotionally appealing**. The thing that makes a stock easy to pitch to friends and family is often the thing that makes it a bad investment.
