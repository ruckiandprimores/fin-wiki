# Divergence

A condition in which price and a technical indicator (typically an oscillator like RSI, MACD, stochastics) move in opposite directions. Divergence signals **momentum exhaustion** — even though price is still moving, the underlying force behind the move is weakening.

## Two main types

### Regular divergence (reversal signal)

| Type | Price action | Oscillator action | Implication |
|------|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Bearish regular** | Higher high | Lower high | Uptrend losing momentum; reversal possible |
| **Bullish regular** | Lower low | Higher low | Downtrend losing momentum; reversal possible |

### Hidden divergence (continuation signal)

| Type | Price action | Oscillator action | Implication |
|------|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Hidden bullish** | Higher low | Lower low | Uptrend pullback; resumption likely |
| **Hidden bearish** | Lower high | Higher high | Downtrend bounce; continuation likely |

## Mechanism

Oscillators measure rate-of-change. When price reaches a new extreme but rate-of-change doesn't, momentum has decelerated. Deceleration precedes (but doesn't always cause) reversal.

## When divergence works best

- After extended trending moves
- At key [[trading/mechanisms/support-resistance|support/resistance]] levels
- Confirmed by [[trading/mechanisms/chart-patterns|chart patterns]] and volume
- Across multiple oscillators (RSI + MACD + stochastics agreeing)

## When divergence fails

- In strong trends, especially mid-trend (oscillators normalize at lower readings; "divergence" can persist for many bars without reversal)
- In choppy markets (every minor swing creates apparent divergence)
- During liquidation cascades in crypto perps (divergence forms then gets blown through by leverage unwinds)
